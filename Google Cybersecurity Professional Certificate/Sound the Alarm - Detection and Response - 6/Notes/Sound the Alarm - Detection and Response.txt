# Incident Response Teams and Security Operations Centers

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) created an Incident Response Lifecycle framework that helps organizations handle cybersecurity incidents effectively. The framework contains four major stages:

* Preparation
* Detection and Analysis
* Containment, Eradication, and Recovery
* Post-Incident Activity

Security teams work together to monitor systems, identify threats, and respond quickly to incidents. Two important groups involved in this process are the Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) and the Security Operations Center (SOC).

## Command, Control, and Communication

A Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) is a group of cybersecurity professionals responsible for managing and responding to security incidents. Effective incident response depends on three important elements:

* **Command:** Leadership and direction during an incident
* **Control:** Managing technical operations, resources, and task assignments
* **Communication:** Keeping all stakeholders informed throughout the incident

Having clearly defined responsibilities allows teams to respond more efficiently and reduce confusion during critical situations.

## Common Roles in a CSIRT

The structure of a CSIRT differs between organizations. Some companies maintain a dedicated response team, while others create temporary task forces when incidents occur. CSIRTs often include both security and nonsecurity personnel such as legal teams, human resources, IT staff, management, and public relations.

### Security Analyst

Security analysts continuously watch systems and networks for suspicious activity. Their duties often include:

* Monitoring and analyzing security alerts
* Investigating the root cause of incidents
* Escalating serious threats to senior team members
* Resolving lower-level alerts when possible

### Technical Lead

Technical leads manage the technical side of incident response. Their responsibilities include:

* Identifying the source of the incident
* Developing strategies for containment and recovery
* Applying patches, updates, or fixes
* Coordinating with other departments to reduce operational disruption

### Incident Coordinator

Incident coordinators help maintain communication between departments during a security incident. Their responsibilities include:

* Coordinating with internal and external teams
* Sharing incident updates with stakeholders
* Ensuring communication remains organized and clear

Depending on the organization, this role may also be called an incident manager or incident commander.

### Additional CSIRT Roles

Some organizations also include specialized positions such as:

* Communications lead
* Legal advisor
* Planning coordinator

## Security Operations Center (SOC)

A Security Operations Center (SOC) is a dedicated team responsible for monitoring systems, networks, and devices for cyber threats. SOC teams are commonly associated with blue team operations, which focus on defending an organization against attacks.

## SOC Structure and Responsibilities

SOC teams are typically organized into different levels based on experience and responsibilities.

### Tier 1 SOC Analyst (L1)

Entry-level analysts handle the initial review of security alerts. Their responsibilities include:

* Monitoring and prioritizing alerts
* Creating and managing tickets in ticketing systems
* Escalating complex incidents to higher tiers

### Tier 2 SOC Analyst (L2)

L2 analysts have more experience and perform deeper investigations. Their responsibilities include:

* Investigating escalated alerts
* Tuning and configuring security tools
* Assisting with incident response activities
* Reporting findings to SOC leadership

### Tier 3 SOC Lead (L3)

SOC leads are senior analysts who oversee technical operations. Their responsibilities include:

* Supervising SOC teams
* Conducting advanced threat detection and analysis
* Performing malware and forensic investigations
* Reporting major findings to management

### SOC Manager

The SOC manager oversees the entire SOC operation. Their responsibilities include:

* Hiring and training team members
* Measuring team performance
* Preparing compliance and incident reports
* Communicating security updates to executives and stakeholders

## Specialized SOC Roles

### Forensic Investigators

These professionals gather and analyze digital evidence to understand how incidents occurred and determine their impact.

### Threat Hunters

Threat hunters proactively search for advanced or hidden threats using threat intelligence and advanced detection methods.

# Detection Tools in Cybersecurity

## Importance of Detection Tools

Detection tools in cybersecurity serve a purpose similar to alarm systems used in homes. Just as home security systems watch for unauthorized entry, cybersecurity detection tools monitor computer systems and networks for suspicious or malicious activity. Organizations rely on these tools to identify potential threats early and reduce the risk of security breaches.

These tools continuously observe network traffic, user behavior, and system events. When unusual activity is identified, they generate alerts so security teams can investigate and respond quickly before major damage occurs.

## Types of Detection Tools

Security analysts commonly work with three major categories of detection technologies:

* Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
* Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS)
* Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)

Each tool provides different capabilities.

| Capability                     | IDS | IPS | EDR |
| ------------------------------ | --- | --- | --- |
| Detects suspicious activity    | ✓   | ✓   | ✓   |
| Prevents attacks automatically | ✗   | ✓   | ✓   |
| Records system activity        | ✓   | ✓   | ✓   |
| Sends security alerts          | ✓   | ✓   | ✓   |
| Uses behavioral analysis       | ✗   | ✗   | ✓   |

---

# Intrusion Detection System (IDS)

An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) monitors systems and network activity for signs of possible attacks or unauthorized behavior. Its primary purpose is to detect suspicious events and notify security personnel.

An IDS only identifies and reports threats; it does not block or stop them. Security analysts must review the alerts and take action if necessary.

For example, if a login attempt occurs from an unfamiliar IP address or at an unusual time, the IDS may generate an alert to warn the security team.

Common IDS tools include:

* Zeek
* Suricata
* Snort
* Sagan

## Detection Outcomes

Security analysts must understand four common alert outcomes generated by detection systems:

### True Positive

A legitimate attack is detected correctly.

### True Negative

No malicious activity exists, and no alert is triggered.

### False Positive

Normal activity is incorrectly flagged as malicious. These alerts waste time because analysts investigate threats that are not real.

### False Negative

Malicious activity occurs, but the system fails to detect it. This is especially dangerous because attacks may continue unnoticed.

---

# Intrusion Prevention System (IPS)

An Intrusion Prevention System (IPS) works similarly to an IDS but includes the ability to actively stop threats.

In addition to detecting suspicious behavior and generating alerts, an IPS can automatically take defensive actions, such as:

* Blocking malicious traffic
* Updating firewall or router rules
* Preventing unauthorized access

For instance, an IPS might detect harmful traffic targeting a server and immediately block the source IP address.

Some tools, such as Suricata, Snort, and Sagan, can function as both IDS and IPS solutions.

---

# Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)

Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) tools focus on monitoring endpoint devices connected to a network. Endpoints include devices such as:

* Desktop computers
* Laptops
* Smartphones
* Tablets

EDR solutions collect and analyze activity data directly from these devices to identify suspicious behavior.

Unlike traditional IDS or IPS tools, EDR platforms use behavioral analysis, machine learning, and artificial intelligence to recognize unusual patterns and advanced threats.

EDR systems can also respond automatically to threats without requiring manual action from security teams.

For example, if an unfamiliar or suspicious process begins running on a workstation, the EDR tool may automatically stop the process to prevent damage.

Examples of EDR tools include:

* Open EDR
* Bitdefender Endpoint Detection and Response
* FortiEDR

# Advantages and Process of SIEM Tools

## Benefits of SIEM Tools

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) tools help organizations collect, manage, and analyze security-related information from multiple systems and devices. These tools improve visibility across networks and support security teams during investigations and incident response.

Some major advantages of SIEM solutions include:

### Access to Security Event Data

SIEM platforms gather data from many devices and systems connected to a network. This includes both historical and real-time event information. Since organizations may operate hundreds of devices, SIEM tools centralize this information so analysts can easily access it from one location.

### Continuous Monitoring and Alerting

SIEM systems constantly monitor network and system activity. They use predefined rules and detection logic to identify suspicious behavior. When activity matches a security rule, the system generates alerts that notify security teams for further investigation.

### Log Retention and Storage

SIEM platforms also serve as centralized storage solutions for logs and event records. Organizations can retain data for different periods depending on compliance, legal, or operational requirements. This stored data can later support investigations, audits, or forensic analysis.

---

# The SIEM Workflow

The operation of a SIEM platform generally follows three important stages:

1. Collecting and aggregating data
2. Normalizing data
3. Analyzing data

These steps allow organizations to gather information from multiple sources, organize it into a usable format, and identify potential threats efficiently.

---

# Step 1: Collecting and Aggregating Data

SIEM tools rely heavily on data collection. During this phase, event logs are gathered from various sources such as:

* Firewalls
* Servers
* Routers
* Endpoints
* Applications

Logs contain useful details including:

* Timestamps
* IP addresses
* User activity
* Device information

Once collected, the logs are combined into a centralized system. This process is known as aggregation. Aggregation removes the need for analysts to manually review logs across multiple systems because all information becomes accessible through the SIEM platform.

## Parsing Data

During collection, SIEM tools may also perform parsing. Parsing extracts important information from raw log data and organizes it into readable fields.

### Example of a Raw Log

```text
April 3 11:01:21 server sshd[1088]: Failed password for user nuhara from 218.124.14.105 port 5023
```

### Example of Parsed Data

```text
host = server
process = sshd
source_user = nuhara
source_ip = 218.124.14.105
source_port = 5023
```

Parsing makes log information easier for analysts and SIEM systems to interpret.

---

# Step 2: Normalizing Data

Because logs come from different devices and software, they often use different formats. For example, firewall logs may look completely different from web server logs.

Normalization converts this data into a standardized structure that the SIEM can process efficiently. Standardized data improves:

* Searching
* Filtering
* Correlation
* Analysis

This step ensures that all event information follows a consistent format regardless of its source.

---

# Step 3: Analyzing Data

After logs are collected and normalized, the SIEM analyzes the information to detect suspicious activity. SIEM platforms use detection rules, conditions, and logic to identify patterns associated with threats or attacks.

If activity matches a detection rule, the system generates an alert for the security team.

## Correlation in SIEM

An important part of SIEM analysis is correlation. Correlation compares multiple log events from different systems to identify relationships or patterns that may indicate malicious activity.

For example, several failed login attempts followed by a successful login from the same IP address could indicate a brute-force attack.

---

Network communication can often be noisy and complex. Everyday activities such as sending emails, streaming videos, or browsing websites all generate network activity in the form of traffic and transmitted data. Network traffic refers to the total volume of data moving across a network, and it may also describe the type of data being transferred, such as HTTP or other protocols. Network data, on the other hand, refers to the actual information that is exchanged between devices connected to a network.

Network monitoring plays a crucial role in maintaining awareness of what is happening across a network at any given time. By gathering and analyzing network traffic, organizations can identify unusual or suspicious behavior that may indicate a security issue. However, effective monitoring requires a clear understanding of what should be observed in the first place. In this section, you will explore why network monitoring is important, the methods used to monitor networks, and the tools that support this process.

Understanding your network

As previously learned, networks enable devices to connect and communicate by exchanging data through defined network protocols. These communications include useful details such as source and destination IP addresses, the amount of data being transferred, timestamps, and additional metadata. Security professionals use this information to build a baseline that represents normal or expected behavior within the network.

A baseline is a reference standard used for comparison purposes. You may already be familiar with similar concepts in daily life. For example, a monthly grocery budget can act as a baseline to track spending patterns and identify unexpected increases. In cybersecurity, baselines are used to define normal behavior for systems, devices, and networks. Once this normal pattern is established, it becomes much easier to recognize deviations that may indicate abnormal or suspicious activity.

Monitoring network activity

After establishing a baseline, the next step is to continuously monitor the network to detect any deviations from expected behavior. Monitoring involves examining different network components to identify unusual patterns, such as unexpectedly large data transfers or irregular communication behavior. Several key areas of a network can be observed for potential malicious activity:

Flow analysis

Flow refers to the movement of data across a network and includes details such as packets, ports, and communication protocols. Packets are transmitted to specific ports, which act as endpoints for sending and receiving data. These ports are usually linked to standard protocols. For instance, port 443 is typically associated with HTTPS, which is used to secure web traffic through encryption.

However, attackers may intentionally use unusual combinations of protocols and ports to avoid detection and maintain hidden communication channels between a compromised system and an external machine. This type of activity is commonly associated with command-and-control (C2) techniques, where attackers remotely manage infected systems. For example, using HTTPS traffic over an uncommon port such as 8088 instead of the standard 443 can be a sign of suspicious behavior. Organizations must therefore understand which ports and protocols are authorized and watch for any inconsistencies or mismatches.

Packet payload analysis

Network packets contain multiple components related to how data is transmitted, including source and destination IP addresses, as well as the payload. The payload represents the actual content being sent across the network. In many cases, this information is encrypted and must be decrypted before it can be fully analyzed. By examining packet payloads, organizations may detect abnormal activity, such as unauthorized transmission of sensitive information outside the network, which could indicate a data exfiltration attempt.

Temporal pattern analysis

Packets also carry time-related information, which helps in analyzing traffic patterns over specific periods. For example, an organization based in North America may typically observe heavy network activity between 9 a.m. and 5 p.m., which forms part of its normal baseline. If unusually large traffic volumes occur outside of these expected working hours, this deviation from the baseline may signal suspicious activity that requires investigation.

By continuously monitoring these patterns, organizations can quickly identify potential intrusions and take action to secure their systems and network infrastructure.

Protecting the network

Throughout this program, you have learned about Security Operations Centers (SOC), which are responsible for detecting, analyzing, and responding to cybersecurity threats. In addition to SOCs, organizations may also operate a Network Operations Center (NOC), which focuses on monitoring network performance and handling issues such as outages or service disruptions. While a SOC is primarily concerned with security monitoring and threat response, a NOC is responsible for ensuring network stability, performance, and availability.

Security analysts observe system and network activity across multiple monitoring screens to identify indicators of compromise (IoCs), which may suggest that a system has been breached or is under attack. To do this effectively, they must understand how normal network communication behaves so they can recognize deviations that may signal malicious activity.

Network monitoring tools

Intrusion Detection Systems are designed to continuously monitor system and network activity and generate alerts when they detect behavior that matches known threat patterns or configured rules. IDS solutions often analyze packet contents, including payload data, to identify signatures associated with threats such as malware, phishing attempts, or other malicious activity.

They allow security professionals to observe communications at a granular level and analyze how data flows across the network. Common examples include tools like tcpdump and Wireshark, which are used to capture packet data for further investigation. These packet captures can then be carefully examined to detect unusual behavior or evidence of potential security incidents.

Security analysts are responsible for observing and analyzing how data moves across a network. A common method for doing this is by creating packet captures and studying the collected traffic to spot unusual or suspicious behavior.

Earlier in the program, you built a foundation in networking concepts. In this section, that knowledge is used to better understand how network traffic flows. The focus here is on three key areas of network analysis: data packets, network protocol analyzers, and packet captures.

Packets

A data packet is the smallest unit of information that is transmitted between devices in a network. Identifying network intrusions often starts at this level because packets are the building blocks of all communication across networks.

Whenever you perform an online activity—such as opening a website or uploading a file—data is broken into multiple packets. These packets travel independently across the network and are then reassembled at the destination device to reconstruct the original data.

In cybersecurity investigations, packets provide essential details that help analysts understand what happened during an event. By studying packet behavior, analysts can detect irregularities and improve their ability to defend systems against attacks.

A packet is generally made up of three main parts: the header, the payload, and the footer.

Header

The header is the first section of a packet and contains critical routing information. Depending on the protocols in use, a packet may include multiple headers such as Ethernet, IP, or TCP headers.

These headers contain details like source and destination IP addresses, packet size, protocol type, and identification numbers. This information helps ensure the packet reaches the correct destination.

Payload

The payload is the section of the packet that carries the actual data being transmitted. For example, if an image is uploaded online, the image data itself is stored in the payload.

Footer

The footer (also called the trailer) appears at the end of certain packets, particularly Ethernet packets. It is mainly used for error detection and helps verify whether the data has been corrupted during transmission. However, many protocols, including IP, do not use a footer.

Network protocol analyzers

Network protocol analyzers, also known as packet sniffers, are tools used to capture and examine network traffic. Common examples include Wireshark, tcpdump, and TShark.

These tools are widely used in cybersecurity to investigate suspicious activity and monitor network behavior. They can also assist in performance analysis, such as measuring bandwidth usage or diagnosing slow network connections.

However, they can also be misused by attackers to intercept sensitive information, including login credentials or confidential data.

The diagram of a typical setup shows data moving between two systems, while a protocol analyzer captures packets as they travel through the network path.

How protocol analyzers function

Protocol analyzers rely on both software and hardware components to collect and display network traffic.

The process begins with the Network Interface Card (NIC), which allows a device to connect to a network. Normally, a NIC only processes traffic addressed to its own device. To capture all visible network traffic, it must be switched into a special mode.

In wired systems, this is often called promiscuous mode, while in wireless networks it is known as monitoring mode. This allows the NIC to observe more network traffic, though not necessarily the entire network. To capture full traffic, the analyzer must be placed at a strategic point in the network.

Once captured, packets are initially stored in binary form (0s and 1s), which is not human-readable. The protocol analyzer converts this data into a readable format so analysts can review and interpret it effectively.

Note that enabling promiscuous mode can introduce security risks, as it may expose sensitive information if misused. Therefore, it should always be used responsibly.

Packet captures

Packet sniffing refers to the process of capturing network traffic for analysis. A packet capture (often called a p-cap) is a file that stores recorded packets from a network interface.

These files can be opened and analyzed using tools like Wireshark or tcpdump. Analysts can filter packet data to focus on specific details, such as traffic from a particular IP address or unusual communication patterns.

It is important to note that capturing network traffic without authorization may be illegal in many regions.

Packet capture files can exist in different formats depending on the system or tool used:

Libpcap is commonly used on Unix-based systems such as Linux and macOS, and is supported by tools like tcpdump.
WinPcap is an older packet capture format used mainly on Windows systems.
Npcap is a modern Windows-compatible capture library developed for tools like Nmap.
PCAPng is an updated format that supports advanced features and can store additional metadata along with packet data.

Internet Protocol (IP) *** Important

Packets are the core unit of communication in any network, meaning that network investigation and detection often begin at the packet level. The Internet Protocol (IP) is a set of rules and standards responsible for addressing and routing these packets as they travel from one device to another across networks. IP serves as the fundamental layer that enables communication over the internet.

The primary role of IP is to ensure that packets successfully reach their intended destination. Today, two versions of IP are commonly used: IPv4 and IPv6. Each version uses a different header structure to organize packet information.

IPv4

IPv4 is the most widely used version of the Internet Protocol. Its header consists of 13 different fields, each serving a specific purpose in packet delivery and routing:

Version: Indicates the IP version being used (IPv4 in this case).
Internet Header Length (IHL): Specifies the size of the IPv4 header, including any optional fields.
Type of Service (ToS): Defines the priority level of the packet for delivery handling.
Total Length: Indicates the full size of the IP packet, including both header and data.
Identification: Assigns a unique value to packets that are fragmented so they can be correctly reassembled at the destination.
Flags: Provides information about fragmentation status, including whether fragmentation has occurred and if more fragments are expected.
Fragment Offset: Determines the correct order of fragmented packet pieces.
Time to Live (TTL): Limits the lifespan of a packet in the network to prevent it from circulating indefinitely.
Protocol: Identifies the protocol used in the data portion of the packet.
Header Checksum: Used for error detection in the header data.
Source Address: Specifies the IP address of the sender.
Destination Address: Specifies the IP address of the receiver.
Options: An optional field that can include additional settings, such as security-related configurations.

An IPv4 header contains these 13 structured fields that help ensure proper delivery and handling of packets.

IPv6

IPv6 is a newer version of the Internet Protocol and is increasingly being adopted due to its significantly larger address space. Unlike IPv4, its header is simpler and contains 8 fields:

Version: Identifies the protocol version (IPv6).
Traffic Class: Similar to IPv4’s Type of Service field, it defines the priority level of the packet.
Flow Label: Identifies packets belonging to the same communication flow, where a flow represents a sequence of packets from a single source.
Payload Length: Indicates the size of the data portion of the packet.
Next Header: Specifies the type of header that follows the IPv6 header, such as TCP.
Hop Limit: Functions similarly to TTL in IPv4, limiting how long a packet can remain in the network before being discarded.
Source Address: Contains the sender’s IP address.
Destination Address: Contains the receiver’s IP address.

An IPv6 header is more streamlined but still carries all essential routing and delivery information.

Header fields in both IPv4 and IPv6 are extremely valuable during investigations, and tools like Wireshark convert this raw data into a readable format for analysis.

Wireshark

Wireshark is an open-source network protocol analyzer that uses a graphical user interface (GUI). This makes it easier for security analysts to visually inspect and interpret network traffic. While Wireshark includes many advanced features, this section focuses on basic filtering techniques used to isolate relevant packets within large packet capture files.

Wireshark interface provides a structured view of captured network data, making it easier to investigate communication patterns.

Display filters in Wireshark

Wireshark display filters allow you to narrow down large packet capture files to only show the data relevant to your investigation. These filters can be applied based on protocols, IP addresses, ports, and many other packet attributes.

Comparison operators

Wireshark supports comparison operators that can be written either as symbols or abbreviations. For example:

Equal: == or eq
Not equal: != or ne
Greater than: > or gt
Less than: < or lt
Greater than or equal to: >= or ge
Less than or equal to: <= or le

For instance, the filter ip.src == 8.8.8.8 is equivalent to ip.src eq 8.8.8.8.

You can also combine these operators using Boolean logic such as and, or, and use parentheses to group conditions for more complex filtering.

Contains operator

The contains operator is used to find packets that include a specific string of text. For example, it can be used to display HTTP traffic that includes the word “moved” within the communication stream.

Matches operator

The matches operator allows filtering using regular expressions (regex). This enables pattern-based searching rather than exact matches. Regular expressions are sequences of characters used to define search patterns, and they are commonly used in advanced filtering scenarios.

Filter toolbar

Wireshark provides a filter toolbar where users can directly enter filter expressions. For example, typing dns will display only DNS-related packets, simplifying analysis of specific protocol traffic.

Wireshark also uses color coding to differentiate protocol types, and users can customize both filters and colors based on preference.

Filtering by protocol

One of the simplest filtering methods is by protocol name. By entering the protocol directly, Wireshark displays only relevant traffic. Common examples include:

dns
http
ftp
ssh
arp
telnet
icmp
Filtering by IP address

Wireshark allows filtering based on IP addresses in several ways:

ip.addr == 172.21.224.2 → filters traffic involving that IP address
ip.src == 10.10.10.10 → filters traffic originating from a specific source
ip.dst == 4.4.4.4 → filters traffic sent to a specific destination
Filtering by MAC address

You can also filter traffic using MAC (Media Access Control) addresses, which uniquely identify network devices. For example:

eth.addr == 00:70:f4:23:18:c4
Filtering by ports

Port-based filtering helps isolate traffic associated with specific services:

udp.port == 53 → filters UDP traffic on port 53 (commonly DNS)
tcp.port == 25 → filters TCP traffic on port 25 (commonly email services)
Following streams

Wireshark includes a “Follow Stream” feature that reconstructs full conversations between two devices. A stream represents a complete exchange of data using a specific protocol.

This feature allows analysts to view the full context of communication, such as HTTP request and response messages, in a readable format rather than individual packets. It is especially useful for understanding how a communication session unfolded from start to finish.
























Tcpdump (IMPORTANT)

As a security analyst, one of your responsibilities is to monitor network traffic and analyze communications moving across a network. Network protocol analyzers are important tools that help identify potential intrusions and investigate suspicious activity. Earlier, you learned the following concepts:

A network protocol analyzer (also called a packet sniffer) is a tool used to capture and inspect network traffic.
Packet sniffing is the process of collecting and examining data packets as they travel through a network.

In this section, you'll take a closer look at tcpdump, a popular network protocol analyzer that allows analysts to capture, inspect, and analyze network communications.

What is tcpdump?

Tcpdump is a command-line network protocol analyzer. A command-line interface (CLI) is a text-based environment where users interact with a system by entering commands instead of using graphical elements.

Tcpdump is primarily used to capture network traffic and analyze the packets being transmitted across a network. Captured traffic can be stored in a packet capture (p-cap) file, which contains intercepted packet data from a network interface. These files can be reviewed immediately, shared with others, or saved for future analysis.

Security professionals use tcpdump for many purposes, including troubleshooting network problems, monitoring traffic patterns, and investigating potentially malicious activity. Tcpdump is included by default in many Linux distributions and can also be installed on other Unix-like operating systems, including macOS.

Note: Much of today's network traffic is encrypted, meaning its contents are encoded and unreadable without proper decryption. Analyzing encrypted packets may require access to the corresponding private keys.

Capturing packets with tcpdump

As discussed previously, Linux systems have a root user (superuser) with elevated permissions. The sudo command temporarily grants these elevated privileges to authorized users.

Because packet capturing requires access to low-level network resources, tcpdump generally must be run with administrator privileges. This means you must either log in as the root user or execute commands using sudo.

The general syntax for capturing packets with tcpdump is:

sudo tcpdump [-i interface] [option(s)] [expression(s)]
Command components

sudo tcpdump

Starts tcpdump with elevated privileges required for packet capturing.

-i interface

Specifies the network interface that tcpdump should monitor. Packet capture cannot begin until an interface is selected.

For example:

sudo tcpdump -i any

The value any instructs tcpdump to listen on all available network interfaces.

option(s)

Options (or flags) modify the behavior of tcpdump and control how packet data is captured or displayed.

expression(s)

Expressions act as filters that help isolate specific traffic of interest. These filters allow analysts to focus on relevant packets during investigations.

Note: Before capturing traffic, it is important to identify available network interfaces. The -D option can be used to display all interfaces on the system.

Tcpdump options

Tcpdump supports numerous options (also called flags) that customize packet capturing and analysis. Short options use a single dash followed by a character, while long options use two dashes followed by the full option name.

Examples:

-i

or

--interface

Tcpdump provides more than fifty available options. The following are among the most commonly used.

The -w option

The -w flag writes captured traffic directly to a packet capture file instead of displaying it on screen.

Example:

sudo tcpdump -i any -w packetcapture.pcap

This command captures traffic from all interfaces and stores it in a file named packetcapture.pcap.

Saving captures is useful because the data can be analyzed later using tcpdump, Wireshark, or other packet analysis tools.

The -r option

The -r flag reads an existing packet capture file.

Example:

sudo tcpdump -r packetcapture.pcap

This command opens and displays packet information from the specified capture file.

The -v option

Packets contain a large amount of information. By default, tcpdump displays only a limited summary.

The verbose option (-v) increases the amount of detail shown.

Tcpdump supports three verbosity levels:

-v
-vv
-vvv

Each additional v increases the level of detail displayed.

Example:

sudo tcpdump -r packetcapture.pcap -v

This command reads the capture file and displays more detailed packet information, including additional header details.

The -c option

The -c flag stands for count and controls how many packets tcpdump captures before stopping.

Examples:

-c 1

Captures one packet.

-c 10

Captures ten packets.

Example command:

sudo tcpdump -i any -c 3

This tells tcpdump to capture only the first three packets observed on all interfaces.

The -n option

By default, tcpdump performs name resolution, converting IP addresses and port numbers into hostnames and service names.

For example:

An IP address may be translated into a domain name.
Port 80 may automatically be displayed as HTTP.

Although convenient, this behavior can sometimes produce misleading results because a port is not always associated with its typical service.

Additionally, name resolution requires reverse DNS lookups. A reverse DNS lookup attempts to identify the hostname associated with an IP address. In some investigations, this could alert an attacker that their infrastructure is being examined.

Using the -n option disables hostname resolution.

Example:

sudo tcpdump -r packetcapture.pcap -v -n

This command reads the capture file, displays verbose information, and prevents hostname lookups.

Using:

-nn

disables both hostname resolution and service-name resolution for ports.

Many analysts consider -n or -nn a best practice during packet analysis.

Pro Tip

Multiple options can often be combined.

For example:

-vn

is equivalent to:

-v -n

However, options that require their own values (such as -c 5 or -r file.pcap) generally cannot be merged with other flags.

Filter expressions

Filter expressions are optional but extremely useful when analyzing large amounts of traffic.

Instead of viewing every packet, expressions allow analysts to focus on specific protocols, IP addresses, ports, or communication types.

For example, to display only IPv6 traffic:

ip6

Tcpdump also supports Boolean operators such as:

and
or
not

These operators can be combined to create more precise filters.

Example:

sudo tcpdump -r packetcapture.pcap -n 'ip and port 80'

This command displays only IPv4 traffic that uses port 80.

Pro Tip

Filter expressions can be enclosed in single or double quotation marks.

Parentheses can also be used to group conditions and define precedence.

Example:

ip and (port 80 or port 443)

This filter first evaluates traffic using ports 80 or 443 and then limits results to IPv4 packets.

Grouping conditions becomes especially valuable when building more complex search criteria.

Understanding tcpdump output

When tcpdump captures packets, it displays one line of output for each packet observed. Every line begins with a timestamp followed by information about the communication.

Consider the following command:

sudo tcpdump -i any -v -c 1

This command:

Captures traffic from all interfaces (-i any)
Displays detailed packet information (-v)
Stops after capturing one packet (-c 1)

The output typically contains the following components:

Timestamp

Each packet begins with a timestamp showing:

Hours
Minutes
Seconds
Fractions of a second

This indicates exactly when the packet was captured.

Source IP Address

The source IP identifies the system that sent the packet.

Source Port

The source port indicates the application or service from which the packet originated.

Destination IP Address

The destination IP identifies the system receiving the packet.

Destination Port

The destination port identifies the service or application to which the packet is being sent.

Additional Packet Details

The remaining output may include:

TCP flags
Sequence numbers
Acknowledgment values
Window sizes
Header information
Other protocol-specific details

When the -v option is used, tcpdump provides even more information about packet headers and communication characteristics, helping analysts better understand network activity and investigate potential security events.

Security analysts rely on detection technologies to uncover potential threats, but there are also other approaches that support identifying malicious activity.

Earlier, you learned how security tools can recognize attacks such as data exfiltration. In this reading, you’ll explore additional detection approaches organizations can use to identify security threats.

Methods of detection

During the Detection and Analysis stage of the incident response lifecycle, security teams receive alerts about possible security incidents and then investigate them by gathering and examining relevant data. In simple terms, detection is about quickly identifying security events, while analysis focuses on confirming and understanding those alerts.

As discussed earlier, an intrusion detection system (IDS) can spot suspicious behavior and generate alerts for analysts to review. Similarly, security information and event management (SIEM) systems help collect, correlate, and analyze security logs to support detection efforts.

However, detection is not perfect. Even strong security teams can miss real attacks for several reasons. Detection tools only work based on what they are configured to monitor, so incorrect or incomplete configuration can allow malicious activity to go unnoticed. Because of this, organizations often combine multiple detection approaches to improve accuracy and coverage.

Threat hunting

Modern attackers constantly change their techniques, making it difficult for automated systems to keep up. Human-led approaches such as threat hunting help bridge this gap by combining analyst expertise with security tools to uncover hidden threats.

Threat hunting is a proactive process where security professionals actively search for malicious activity within a network instead of waiting for alerts. It is also used to investigate suspicious behavior in more depth and identify threats before they cause serious harm. For example, fileless malware is especially hard to detect because it operates in system memory instead of relying on files, helping it evade traditional signature-based detection. Threat hunting helps uncover such stealthy threats through manual investigation supported by tools.

Note: Individuals who perform threat hunting are called threat hunters. They study emerging attack methods and evaluate how likely an organization is to be targeted. Their work often uses threat intelligence, indicators of compromise (IOCs), indicators of attack (IOAs), and machine learning techniques to locate hidden threats.

Threat intelligence

Organizations can strengthen detection by staying informed about current and emerging threats and understanding how attackers may target their systems. This is done using threat intelligence, which refers to analyzed, evidence-based information about potential or active threats.

Threat intelligence is gathered from several sources, including:

Industry research reports: These describe attacker behavior, including tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).
Government alerts: These also provide insights into attacker methods and TTPs.
Threat feeds: These continuously deliver data such as suspicious IP addresses, domains, and file hashes, which may be linked to advanced persistent threats (APTs), where attackers maintain long-term unauthorized access to systems.

Managing large amounts of this information can be difficult, so organizations often use a threat intelligence platform (TIP). A TIP collects, organizes, and analyzes threat data from multiple sources, helping security teams prioritize risks and improve defenses.

Note: Threat feeds should support detection by adding context, not be used as the sole basis for creating detection rules.

Cyber deception

Cyber deception techniques are designed to intentionally mislead attackers in order to detect them and strengthen defensive capabilities.

One common example is a honeypot, which is a deliberately exposed decoy system meant to attract attackers. For instance, a fake file named Client Credit Card Information - 2022 can be used to lure malicious users. When an attacker interacts with it, security teams receive alerts, allowing them to observe behavior and respond accordingly.

Understanding the Pyramid of Pain and Indicators of Compromise

This reading introduces the concept of the Pyramid of Pain, a framework that helps security teams understand the effectiveness of different indicators used to detect malicious activity. By understanding how attackers react when these indicators are blocked, organizations can strengthen their defenses and reduce the impact of security incidents.

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) are pieces of observable evidence that suggest a security breach or malicious activity may have occurred. They provide clues that an attack has taken place and help investigators identify what happened.

Examples of IoCs include:

A malicious file name
A suspicious IP address
A known malware hash value
An unauthorized domain connection

An IoC can be compared to discovering signs of a completed crime, such as noticing that valuables have been stolen from a vehicle. The evidence points to an event that has already happened.

Indicators of Attack (IoAs)

Indicators of Attack (IoAs) focus on identifying attacker behavior while an attack is occurring. Rather than highlighting evidence left behind, IoAs reveal suspicious actions and patterns that indicate malicious intent.

IoAs help security teams understand:

How an attack is being carried out
Why the attacker is performing certain actions
What techniques are being used

For example:

A process establishing an unexpected network connection is an IoA.
The process name and destination IP address associated with that connection are IoCs.

In simple terms:

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)	Indicators of Attack (IoAs)
Show evidence of a completed or existing compromise	Show attacker behavior during an attack
Focus on "who" and "what"	Focus on "how" and "why"
Often discovered after an event	Often detected during an event

Note: An IoC alone does not always confirm a security incident. Similar indicators may result from user mistakes, software errors, or system issues unrelated to malicious activity.

The Pyramid of Pain

Not all indicators provide the same level of value to defenders. Some indicators are easy for attackers to change, while others require significant effort to modify.

To illustrate this concept, security researcher David J. Bianco developed the Pyramid of Pain.

The Pyramid of Pain demonstrates how difficult it becomes for attackers to continue their operations when different types of indicators are detected and blocked. As defenders move higher up the pyramid, attackers experience greater disruption and must invest more effort to evade detection.

The lower levels cause minimal inconvenience, while the upper levels can significantly impact an attacker's ability to operate.

Levels of the Pyramid of Pain
1. Hash Values (Least Painful)

Hash values are unique identifiers generated from files.

Examples:

Malware file hashes
Hashes of malicious documents
Hashes linked to compromised files

Security teams often use hash values to identify known malicious files quickly.

Attacker Difficulty: Low

Attackers can simply modify a file slightly to generate a completely different hash value.

2. IP Addresses

An IP address identifies a device on a network.

Example:

192.168.1.1

Blocking malicious IP addresses can prevent communication with known attacker infrastructure.

Attacker Difficulty: Low

Attackers can easily switch to different IP addresses, cloud resources, or proxy services.

3. Domain Names

Domain names are human-readable web addresses.

Examples:

example.com
malicious-domain.net

Security teams often block domains associated with phishing campaigns or malware distribution.

Attacker Difficulty: Moderate

Registering a new domain is relatively inexpensive and straightforward for attackers.

4. Network Artifacts

Network artifacts are traces of malicious activity that appear within network traffic.

Examples include:

Suspicious User-Agent strings
Unusual HTTP headers
Abnormal protocol behavior
Distinct communication patterns

These artifacts often reveal attacker techniques rather than specific infrastructure.

Attacker Difficulty: Moderate to High

Changing network behavior usually requires modifications to malware or attack tools.

5. Host Artifacts

Host artifacts are traces left behind on systems targeted by attackers.

Examples:

Malware-created files
Registry modifications
Scheduled tasks
Persistence mechanisms

These indicators often reveal how malware operates on a compromised device.

Attacker Difficulty: High

Attackers must redesign portions of their malware or attack process to avoid detection.

6. Tools

Tools are the software applications or utilities attackers use to achieve their objectives.

Examples:

Password-cracking programs
Remote access tools
Malware frameworks
Exploitation utilities

A common example is the password-cracking tool John the Ripper.

Attacker Difficulty: Very High

Replacing or modifying tools requires time, expertise, and additional resources.

7. Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) (Most Painful)

At the top of the pyramid are Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs).

Tactics describe an attacker's overall objective.
Techniques explain how the objective is achieved.
Procedures provide the detailed steps used during execution.

Examples:

Credential dumping
Lateral movement
Privilege escalation
Phishing campaigns
Persistence techniques

TTPs represent attacker behavior rather than specific artifacts.

Attacker Difficulty: Extremely High

When defenders detect and block TTPs, attackers may need to redesign significant portions of their operations, making it much harder to continue their attacks successfully.

























Investigative Tools and Adding Context to Security Incidents

In previous lessons, you explored various detection methods used to identify security incidents. This reading focuses on how investigative tools help security analysts examine suspicious Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) and gather additional context around alerts. Remember, an IoC is any observable piece of evidence that may indicate a potential security incident.

Why Context Matters in Investigations

The Pyramid of Pain demonstrates that different IoCs provide different levels of value during threat detection. While IoCs help identify suspicious activity, relying on a single indicator often provides only a limited view of an attack.

For example, blocking a malicious IP address may stop one aspect of an attack, but it does not reveal the attacker's overall objectives, methods, or related activities. Attackers can often switch to a different IP address and continue their operations.

Focusing on a single IoC is similar to examining only one small section of a large picture—you may miss important details needed to understand the entire situation.

To gain a more complete understanding, security analysts use threat intelligence, which is evidence-based information about current or emerging threats. Threat intelligence provides additional context around IoCs and helps analysts connect related artifacts, activities, and behaviors.

For instance, a suspicious IP address may be linked to:

Unusual network communications
Malicious domains
Suspicious files
Abnormal system processes

By connecting these indicators, security teams can build a clearer picture of the incident, understand its scope, and make better-informed response decisions.

In cybersecurity, crowdsourcing allows organizations to share threat intelligence with the broader security community. Traditionally, security incidents were investigated in isolation, with each organization handling attacks independently. Security researchers

The Role of Documentation in Cybersecurity

Documentation refers to any recorded information created for a specific purpose, and it plays a vital role in cybersecurity operations. Security teams rely on documentation to support investigations, guide daily tasks, and communicate findings clearly across the organization.

This section explores the key benefits of documentation and outlines best practices for creating effective and reliable security documentation.

Benefits of Documentation

Effective security documentation provides three main advantages:

Transparency

Transparency is essential in cybersecurity, especially for compliance, audits, insurance requirements, and legal processes. Proper documentation ensures that every action taken during an incident can be traced and verified.

A key example is the chain of custody, which is the process of recording the handling, transfer, and control of evidence throughout an incident investigation. This creates a clear audit trail and ensures accountability at every stage.

Standardization

Standardization refers to the use of consistent procedures and structured processes across security operations. It helps organizations improve efficiency, support knowledge sharing, and simplify onboarding for new team members.

Organizations often rely on security frameworks such as those developed by NIST to guide best practices.

A common example of standardized documentation is an incident response plan. This document outlines predefined steps for handling security incidents, ensuring that teams follow consistent procedures during investigations and response efforts. Standardization ensures that responses are repeatable, organized, and aligned with organizational goals.

Clarity

Clear documentation ensures that information is easy to understand and actionable for its intended audience. In cybersecurity, analysts must clearly explain the reasoning behind decisions such as escalating or closing alerts.

Well-structured documentation allows team members to quickly access relevant information and take appropriate action without confusion or delay.

Best Practices for Effective Security Documentation

To create strong and useful documentation in cybersecurity, professionals should follow these key practices:

1. Understand Your Audience

Before writing any document, it is important to identify who will read it and what level of detail they require.

For example:

A SOC analyst or security manager expects technical details and structured analysis.
A CEO or executive audience requires high-level summaries without technical jargon.

Tailoring documentation to the audience ensures better understanding and usability.

2. Keep It Concise

Documentation should be clear and focused. Overly long or complex documents may discourage people from reading or using them effectively.

The purpose of the document should be stated early, often in an executive summary. This helps readers quickly understand the key points and relevance of the information.

Concise writing improves accessibility and ensures important details are not overlooked.

3. Maintain Regular Updates

Cybersecurity environments change rapidly due to new vulnerabilities, threats, and attack techniques. As a result, documentation must be continuously reviewed and updated.

After an incident is resolved, teams should conduct post-incident reviews to identify gaps in processes or improvements needed in procedures. Updating documentation ensures that security practices remain effective and aligned with current threats.

Understanding the Security Triage Process

Previously, you learned that triage is used in cybersecurity to evaluate alerts and assign them a level of priority. In this section, you will explore how the triage process works and why it is important for security operations. As a security analyst, triaging alerts efficiently helps ensure that critical threats are handled quickly and effectively.

What is Triage?

Triage is the process of categorizing security incidents based on their urgency and importance. Since organizations may receive large volumes of alerts, triage helps security teams decide which issues need immediate attention and which can be handled later.

This ensures that limited time and resources are focused on the most serious threats first.

Steps in the Triage Process

The triage process typically includes three main stages:

1. Receive and Assess

In this step, a security analyst receives an alert from a monitoring system such as an intrusion detection system (IDS), which tracks network or system activity and generates alerts for suspicious behavior.

The analyst then evaluates the alert to understand its context and determine whether it represents a real security concern.

Key evaluation questions include:

Is this a false positive, or a legitimate threat?
Has this alert occurred before, and how was it handled previously?
Is the alert linked to a known vulnerability or exploit?
How severe is the alert in terms of potential impact?

This stage is focused on gathering initial information and confirming whether the alert requires further investigation.

2. Assign Priority

Once an alert is confirmed as valid, it must be prioritized based on its potential impact. Not all incidents affect systems or organizations equally, so prioritization helps ensure that the most critical issues are addressed first.

Key factors used to determine priority include:

Functional Impact

This refers to how the incident affects the operation of systems and services. For example, a ransomware attack may disrupt access to systems by encrypting or deleting data, severely affecting availability and functionality.

Information Impact

This focuses on how the incident affects data confidentiality, integrity, and availability. For instance, data theft can expose sensitive personal or organizational information and impact third parties as well.

Recoverability

This considers how easily an organization can recover from the incident. Some incidents may be reversible, while others—such as leaked proprietary data—may be impossible to fully recover from. The effort and resources required for recovery also influence priority decisions.

Many security alerts already include a severity rating, which helps guide prioritization.

3. Collect and Analyze

In this final stage, the analyst gathers detailed evidence from multiple sources and performs in-depth analysis to better understand the incident.

This may involve:

Reviewing system and network logs
Conducting external threat research
Correlating related alerts and events
Documenting findings and actions taken

The goal is to build enough context to make an informed decision about the incident. Depending on its severity, the alert may be escalated to a more experienced analyst or a security manager for further handling.

Benefits of the Triage Process

Effective triage provides several important advantages for security teams:

Efficient Resource Allocation

Triage ensures that analysts focus their time and effort on high-priority threats instead of spending excessive time on low-risk alerts. This improves response efficiency and reduces delays in handling critical incidents.

Consistent and Standardized Response

Triage follows structured procedures, often supported by documentation such as incident response playbooks. This ensures that alerts are handled consistently, validated properly, and escalated only when necessary.

Business Continuity Planning (BCP)

Security teams must be ready to reduce the impact that security incidents can have on normal business operations. When disruptions occur, organizations may face serious interruptions to their systems and services. If these disruptions last for long periods, they can lead to financial loss, legal issues, and damage to reputation.

To handle this, organizations use business continuity planning to ensure they can continue operating even during major incidents.

What is a Business Continuity Plan?

A Business Continuity Plan (BCP) is a documented strategy that defines how an organization will maintain essential operations during and after a significant disruption.

Its main purpose is to ensure that critical business functions can either continue without interruption or be restored quickly after an incident.

While entry-level security analysts are usually not responsible for creating or testing BCPs, understanding them is important because they play a key role in incident response and recovery.

BCP vs Disaster Recovery Plan

A Business Continuity Plan (BCP) is not the same as a disaster recovery plan.

BCP: Focuses on keeping business operations running during and after a disruption.
Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP): Focuses specifically on restoring IT systems and data after a major failure or disaster (such as hardware failure or natural events like floods).

Both work together but serve different purposes.

Impact of Security Incidents on Business Continuity

Security incidents such as ransomware attacks can severely disrupt business operations. In critical sectors like healthcare, the impact can be especially serious.

For example, ransomware may encrypt important systems and block access to medical records. This can prevent healthcare providers from delivering timely treatment and disrupt essential services.

At a larger scale, attacks on critical infrastructure can affect:

Public safety
National security
Economic stability
Essential services

Because of these risks, business continuity planning is essential to ensure that important services remain available even during attacks.

Recovery Strategies in Business Continuity

To restore operations after a disruption, organizations use recovery strategies defined in their BCP. These strategies help systems return to normal functioning as quickly as possible.

One important area of recovery is site resilience, which focuses on ensuring that infrastructure such as data centers and networks remain available during failures or attacks.

Site Resilience and Recovery Sites

Site resilience refers to an organization’s ability to prepare for, respond to, and recover from disruptions while continuing operations.

To support this, organizations use different types of backup sites:

Hot Sites

A hot site is a fully operational backup environment that mirrors the primary system.

Ready for immediate use
Fully synchronized with primary infrastructure
Activated instantly during a failure
Warm Sites

A warm site is partially prepared and contains updated systems and configurations.

Not fully operational at all times
Requires some setup before use
Can be activated relatively quickly
Cold Sites

A cold site provides only basic infrastructure.

Not immediately operational
Requires significant setup after a disruption
Least expensive option, but slowest recovery time

Post-Incident Activity in the Incident Response Lifecycle

Earlier, you learned about the Containment, Eradication, and Recovery phase of the incident response process. This section focuses on the final stage of the NIST Incident Response Lifecycle: Post-Incident Activity.

This phase is dedicated to reviewing the incident, identifying lessons learned, and improving future incident response efforts. Every security incident provides valuable insights that can help strengthen an organization's security posture and response capabilities.

What is Post-Incident Activity?

The Post-Incident Activity phase occurs after an incident has been successfully contained, eliminated, and resolved. During this stage, organizations evaluate the entire incident handling process to determine what worked well and what could be improved.

Rather than simply closing the case, security teams use this opportunity to enhance procedures, technologies, and response strategies for future incidents.

Lessons Learned Meetings

One of the most important activities during this phase is conducting a lessons learned meeting, often referred to as a post-mortem review.

These meetings bring together everyone involved in responding to the incident. Their purpose is to review the incident from start to finish, assess response efforts, and identify opportunities for improvement.

The focus of these discussions is improvement and learning—not assigning blame.

Organizations typically conduct these meetings within two weeks after the incident has been fully resolved.

When Are Lessons Learned Meetings Needed?

Not every security incident requires a dedicated review meeting.

The decision often depends on:

Incident severity
Scope of impact
Business disruption
Regulatory requirements

Major incidents, such as ransomware attacks or significant data breaches, generally warrant a formal lessons learned session.

Common Questions Discussed

During a lessons learned meeting, teams often examine questions such as:

What occurred during the incident?
When did the incident begin?
How was it detected?
Who identified the issue?
How was the threat contained?
What recovery actions were performed?
What challenges were encountered?
What could have been done more effectively?

Answering these questions helps organizations understand both strengths and weaknesses in their response process.

Preparing for a Lessons Learned Meeting

Effective meetings require preparation.

Common best practices include:

Distributing an agenda before the meeting
Sharing relevant documentation beforehand
Assigning a moderator to guide discussions
Designating a scribe to record notes and action items
Ensuring all participants come prepared with relevant information

This structure helps maximize the value of the review process.

Developing Recommendations

A key outcome of a lessons learned meeting is the creation of actionable recommendations.

During the review, teams may discover:

Gaps in response procedures
Communication issues
Insufficient security controls
Delays in detection or escalation
Missing documentation
Ineffective tools or technologies

These findings are converted into prioritized recommendations that help strengthen future incident response efforts.

Examples of improvements include:

Updating incident response playbooks
Enhancing monitoring and alerting systems
Improving analyst training
Implementing additional security tools
Revising communication procedures

The goal is to reduce the likelihood of experiencing similar incidents in the future.

Final Incident Report

Another critical activity in the post-incident phase is creating a final incident report.

Throughout an investigation, security teams document evidence, actions taken, findings, and decisions. This information is compiled into a comprehensive report once the incident is closed.

The final report serves as the official record of the incident and provides valuable information for management, auditors, compliance teams, and future investigations.

Key Components of a Final Report

Although report formats vary between organizations, most final reports contain several common sections.

Executive Summary

A concise overview of the incident that highlights:

Major findings
Impact on the organization
Key response actions
Overall outcome

This section is often intended for executives and decision-makers.

Incident Timeline

A chronological record of important events, including:

Initial detection
Escalation activities
Investigation milestones
Containment actions
Recovery efforts
Incident closure

Accurate timestamps help reconstruct the sequence of events.

Investigation Details

This section documents the technical investigation and analysis performed during the incident.

Examples include:

Log analysis
Network traffic investigations
Malware analysis
Evidence collection
Threat intelligence findings

It explains how analysts determined the nature and scope of the incident.

Recommendations

The report concludes with recommended actions designed to improve future security operations.

These recommendations often originate from lessons learned discussions and focus on preventing similar incidents from occurring again.

The Importance of Audience Awareness

When preparing a final report, it is important to consider who will read it.

Different audiences require different levels of detail:

Security analysts may need technical findings and evidence.
Managers may need operational summaries.
Executives often prefer business-focused overviews with minimal technical jargon.

Tailoring the report to its audience improves communication and ensures that key information is understood by all stakeholders.

Indicators of Compromise (IoC): Evidence that may indicate a system has been targeted, compromised, or involved in malicious activity.
