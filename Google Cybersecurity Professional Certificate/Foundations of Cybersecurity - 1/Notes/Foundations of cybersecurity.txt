Key Cybersecurity Terms and Concepts

Compliance: The process of following internal policies and external regulations to help organizations avoid legal penalties, security incidents, and data breaches.

Security frameworks: Structured guidelines that help organizations create strategies and procedures to reduce risks and protect data and privacy.

Security controls: Protective measures or safeguards designed to minimize specific security risks and strengthen an organization’s overall security posture.

Security posture: An organization’s overall ability to protect critical systems, assets, and data while adapting to changing threats and security challenges. A stronger security posture generally results in lower risk.

Threat actor: Any individual or group that poses a security risk to systems, networks, applications, or data. Threat actors may intentionally attempt to exploit vulnerabilities or gain unauthorized access.

Internal threat: A security risk caused by someone within or closely connected to the organization, such as employees, vendors, or trusted partners. Internal threats may be accidental, like clicking a malicious link, or intentional, such as unauthorized access to sensitive information.

Network security: The practice of protecting an organization’s network infrastructure, systems, devices, and data from unauthorized access and cyber threats.

Cloud security: The process of securing cloud-based systems, applications, and data by ensuring proper configurations and restricting access to authorized users only. Cloud security focuses on protecting resources stored in remote data centers accessible through the internet.

Programming: The process of creating instructions that allow computers to perform specific tasks. In cybersecurity, programming is commonly used for automation, analyzing web traffic, detecting suspicious activity, and improving security operations.

This helps cybersecurity professionals identify and analyze potential security threats, risks, and vulnerabilities more efficiently.

Intrusion detection systems (IDSs): Cybersecurity analysts use IDSs to monitor system activity and alerts for possible intrusions. It’s important to become familiar with IDSs because they’re a key tool that every organization uses to protect assets and data. For example, you might use an IDS to monitor networks for signs of malicious activity, like unauthorized access to a network.

Threat landscape knowledge: Being aware of current trends related to threat actors, malware, or threat methodologies is vital. This knowledge allows security teams to build stronger defenses against threat actor tactics and techniques. By staying up to date on attack trends and patterns, security professionals are better able to recognize when new types of threats emerge such as a new ransomware variant.

Phishing

Phishing is a cyberattack technique that uses digital communication to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information or installing malicious software. Attackers often impersonate trusted sources to gain access to personal or organizational data.

Common types of phishing attacks include:

Business Email Compromise (BEC): An attack where threat actors impersonate a trusted individual or organization through email to request sensitive information or financial transactions.

Spear phishing: A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or group. The message is carefully designed to appear trustworthy and legitimate.

Whaling: A specialized form of spear phishing that targets executives or high-level officials to gain access to valuable or confidential information.

Vishing: A phishing technique that uses voice communication, such as phone calls, to manipulate victims into sharing sensitive information.

Smishing: A phishing attack carried out through text messages to deceive users into revealing private information or clicking malicious links.

Malware

Malware refers to software intentionally created to damage systems, steal data, disrupt operations, or gain unauthorized access to devices and networks. Threat actors commonly use malware for financial gain, espionage, or cyber sabotage.

Common types of malware include:

Viruses: Malicious programs that attach themselves to files or software. They activate when users open infected files and can corrupt or destroy data.

Worms: Self-replicating malware capable of spreading across networks without user interaction. Worms can quickly infect multiple systems within an organization.

Ransomware: A type of malware that encrypts an organization’s files or systems and demands payment in exchange for restoring access.

Spyware: Malware designed to secretly collect user information without consent. Spyware may capture emails, messages, browsing activity, passwords, locations, or recordings.

Social Engineering

Social engineering is a manipulation technique that exploits human behavior and trust to gain access to sensitive information, systems, or valuables. Instead of attacking systems directly, threat actors target people by creating deceptive situations.

Common types of social engineering attacks include:

Social media phishing: Attackers gather personal information from social media platforms before launching targeted attacks.

Watering hole attack: A threat actor compromises a website frequently visited by a specific group in order to infect its visitors.

USB baiting: An attack where infected USB devices are intentionally left in public places, hoping someone will connect them to a system.

Physical social engineering: A threat actor impersonates an employee, vendor, or customer to gain unauthorized physical access to a restricted area.

Principles of Social Engineering

Social engineering attacks are effective because they exploit common human behaviors such as trust, fear, curiosity, and urgency. Attackers rely on psychological manipulation rather than technical vulnerabilities.

Common tactics used in social engineering include:

Authority: Threat actors pretend to be individuals in positions of power because people are more likely to follow instructions from authority figures.

Intimidation: Attackers use pressure, fear, or threats to force victims into making quick decisions.

Consensus/Social proof: Threat actors convince victims that others have already trusted or approved their requests, making the situation appear legitimate.

Scarcity: Attackers create a false sense of limited availability or urgency to pressure victims into acting quickly.

Familiarity: Threat actors build emotional connections or appear friendly to gain trust more easily.

Trust: Attackers gradually develop relationships with victims to manipulate them over time.

Urgency: Threat actors pressure individuals to act immediately without carefully thinking or verifying the request.

Attack Types

Password Attack

A password attack is an attempt to gain unauthorized access to password-protected systems, devices, networks, or accounts. Attackers use different techniques to crack or steal passwords in order to access sensitive information.

Common password attack methods include:

Brute force attack: A method where attackers repeatedly try different password combinations until the correct one is found.

Rainbow table attack: A technique that uses precomputed tables of encrypted passwords to crack password hashes more efficiently.

Password attacks are generally associated with the communication and network security domain.

Social Engineering Attack

A social engineering attack manipulates human behavior and trust to obtain sensitive information, access, or valuables. Instead of targeting systems directly, attackers exploit human error and psychological weaknesses.

Common forms of social engineering attacks include:

Phishing

Smishing

Vishing

Spear phishing

Whaling

Social media phishing

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Watering hole attacks

USB baiting

Physical social engineering

These attacks are commonly linked to the security and risk management domain.

Physical Attack

A physical attack is a security incident that targets physical devices, environments, or infrastructure in addition to digital systems.

Examples of physical attacks include:

Malicious USB cables

Malicious flash drives

Card cloning and skimming

Physical attacks are related to the asset security domain.

Adversarial Artificial Intelligence

Adversarial artificial intelligence involves manipulating artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) technologies to improve the effectiveness of cyberattacks. Threat actors may exploit AI systems to bypass security controls, automate attacks, or manipulate data.

This attack type is connected to both the communication and network security domain and the identity and access management domain.

Supply-Chain Attack

A supply-chain attack targets vulnerabilities within third-party systems, hardware, software, or services connected to an organization. Since products and services often involve multiple vendors and providers, attackers may compromise one part of the supply chain to gain broader access.

These attacks can affect multiple organizations at once and often result in significant financial and operational damage.

Supply-chain attacks are associated with several security domains, including:

Security and risk management

Security architecture and engineering

Security operations

Cryptographic Attack

A cryptographic attack targets secure communication methods and encryption systems in order to intercept, alter, or decrypt sensitive information exchanged between parties.

Common cryptographic attack methods include:

Birthday attack

Collision attack

Downgrade attack

Cryptographic attacks fall under the communication and network security domain.



Threat actor types

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)
APTs are highly skilled attackers who gain unauthorized access to systems and remain hidden for long periods. They usually plan attacks in advance and carefully study their targets, which are often large organizations or government systems. Their goals can include stealing sensitive intellectual property, disrupting critical infrastructure, or long-term espionage.

Insider threats
Insider threats come from individuals within or connected to an organization, such as employees, contractors, or trusted partners. Since they already have some level of access, they can misuse it intentionally or accidentally. Common motivations include sabotage, data theft, corruption, or leaking confidential information.

Hacktivists
Hacktivists are attackers driven by political or social causes. Instead of financial gain, they use hacking techniques to promote ideologies, protest organizations, spread propaganda, or draw attention to specific issues.

Types of hackers
Hackers are individuals who use technical skills to access systems, networks, or data. They are generally grouped into three categories:

Authorized (ethical) hackers: These professionals work legally to identify and fix security weaknesses. Their goal is to improve security and protect systems.

Semi-authorized hackers (security researchers): They explore vulnerabilities and security flaws but do not exploit them for harm.

Unauthorized (malicious) hackers: These attackers break into systems illegally, often to steal data, cause damage, or make financial profit.

Other hacker motivations
Some inexperienced attackers explore hacking to learn skills or test tools, while others act out of revenge or curiosity. There are also individuals hired to perform hacking tasks, which may fall into ethical or unethical categories depending on intent and authorization. Additionally, some self-described “vigilante hackers” try to target malicious actors, although this often operates outside legal boundaries.

Security controls are safeguards used to reduce risks and protect systems, data, and users. They are implemented within organizations to enforce security policies and are often guided by security frameworks.

Defining security objectives
Creating policies and procedures
Implementing security controls
Monitoring and improving security posture

Ethical Concerns and Laws Related to Counterattacks

United States Perspective
In the United States, launching a counterattack against a cyber threat actor is considered illegal under laws such as the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) and the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA). Cybersecurity professionals are only permitted to defend systems, not retaliate.

Counterattacks are considered a form of vigilantism, where individuals attempt to take justice into their own hands without legal authority. Since attackers are often unpredictable, retaliation can escalate the situation, causing greater damage or wider impact.

In cases involving state-sponsored attackers or politically motivated groups (hacktivists), counterattacks can also create international legal and diplomatic risks. For this reason, only authorized government or military personnel are permitted to conduct offensive cyber operations.

International Perspective
International guidelines, such as those referenced by global legal frameworks like the International Court of Justice (ICJ), suggest that counteractions may only be considered under very strict conditions:

The action must target only the original attacker
It must serve as a direct warning or request to stop the attack
It must not escalate the conflict
Any impact should be reversible

In practice, these conditions are extremely difficult to guarantee. As a result, organizations generally avoid counterattacks due to legal uncertainty, operational risk, and potential for unintended harm.

Entry-Level Analyst Toolkit

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Tools
SIEM tools are applications used to collect, process, and analyze log data from different systems within an organization. A log is a record of events generated by systems, applications, or networks.

Instead of manually reviewing large amounts of data, SIEM tools automatically filter and highlight important events such as security threats, unusual behavior, or system vulnerabilities. They also provide dashboards that organize security data visually, making it easier for analysts to investigate incidents.

SIEM tools may be deployed in different environments:

On-premise systems, managed within an organization’s infrastructure
Cloud-based systems, which are easier to deploy and maintain

Organizations choose between these based on cost, expertise, and operational needs.

Network Protocol Analyzers (Packet Sniffers)
A network protocol analyzer is a tool used to capture and examine network traffic. It helps security analysts understand what data is moving across a network and detect suspicious or unauthorized activity.

These tools are essential for identifying network issues, investigating attacks, and monitoring communication between systems.

Security Playbooks
A playbook is a structured guide that outlines how to respond to specific security situations. It provides step-by-step procedures that help analysts handle incidents consistently and correctly.

Organizations use different playbooks depending on the situation, but their main purpose is to ensure proper and standardized responses to security events.

Two important types of playbooks are:

Chain of Custody Playbook
This playbook defines how digital evidence should be documented and tracked during an investigation. It ensures that every piece of evidence is recorded, including who handled it, when it was accessed, and where it was stored. Maintaining a clear chain of custody ensures that evidence remains valid and legally acceptable.

Protecting and Preserving Evidence Playbook
This playbook focuses on handling digital evidence carefully to prevent alteration or loss. It is especially important for volatile data, which can disappear if a system is turned off. Analysts follow the principle of order of volatility, which prioritizes collecting the most fragile data first.

Proper evidence handling often includes creating copies of data before analysis to ensure original information remains unchanged and usable throughout an investigation.

Key Idea
Entry-level cybersecurity analysts rely on tools like SIEM systems, packet analyzers, and structured playbooks to detect threats, investigate incidents, and handle evidence in a consistent and reliable manner.
