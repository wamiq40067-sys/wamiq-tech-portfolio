Understanding Risk, Threats, and Vulnerabilities in Cybersecurity

When a security incident happens, cybersecurity teams must communicate clearly and work together quickly to reduce damage and restore systems. To do that effectively, professionals rely on three important concepts: risk, threats, and vulnerabilities. Although these terms are often used casually in daily conversation, they have specific meanings in cybersecurity.

What is Security Risk?

A risk refers to anything that could affect the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an organization’s assets. Different organizations view risk differently because each business values different assets and faces different challenges.

Risk is commonly evaluated using the following idea:

Risk=Likelihood×Impact

This means that risk depends on:

How likely an event is to happen
How severe the consequences would be if it occurs

For example, imagine commuting to work by car. Getting a flat tire increases the chance of arriving late. If being late has serious consequences, such as disciplinary action or job loss, the overall risk becomes more significant.

In cybersecurity, risk assessment helps organizations:

Avoid expensive disruptions
Improve security processes and systems
Decide which risks are acceptable
Focus protection efforts on the most valuable assets

Security professionals often concentrate on reducing the likelihood of incidents by identifying and controlling factors that contribute to risk.

Main Factors That Contribute to Risk

Two major elements strongly influence cybersecurity risk:

Threats
Vulnerabilities

Risk becomes more serious when a threat is able to take advantage of a vulnerability.

Using the flat tire example again:

The sharp nail on the road represents the threat
The tire’s ability to be punctured is the vulnerability

Reducing exposure to nails by choosing safer roads lowers the overall risk.

Types of Threats

A threat is any event or condition that could harm systems, data, or operations. Threats are usually divided into two categories:

1. Intentional Threats

These are deliberate attacks or harmful actions carried out by individuals or groups.

Example:

A hacker exploiting a poorly configured application to steal sensitive information
2. Unintentional Threats

These occur accidentally and are not meant to cause harm.

Example:

An employee unknowingly allowing an unauthorized person into a restricted area

Both intentional and accidental threats can create serious security incidents.

Types of Vulnerabilities

A vulnerability is a weakness that can be exploited by a threat. Vulnerabilities generally fall into two categories:

1. Technical Vulnerabilities

These involve weaknesses in systems, software, or hardware.

Example:

Incorrectly configured software that exposes sensitive data
2. Human Vulnerabilities

These involve mistakes or unsafe actions by people.

Example:

An employee misplacing an access badge in a public area

Both technical and human weaknesses can increase the chances of a security breach.

Relationship Between Risk, Threats, and Vulnerabilities

These three concepts are closely connected:

A threat is something that can cause harm
A vulnerability is a weakness that allows harm to happen
Risk is the possibility and impact of that harm occurring

Cybersecurity teams work to reduce risk by:

Identifying threats
Fixing vulnerabilities
Improving security controls and awareness

Understanding these concepts is essential for protecting organizational assets and responding effectively to security events.

Introduction to Cloud Computing and Cloud Security

Launching and running an online business was once expensive and technically difficult. Organizations previously needed to purchase, manage, and maintain their own infrastructure to support online operations. Today, cloud computing has made digital services far more accessible and affordable for businesses of all sizes.

Cloud technologies allow organizations to scale operations quickly, reduce infrastructure costs, and improve flexibility. However, moving services and data to the cloud has also introduced new cybersecurity risks and security management challenges.

What are Cloud-Based Services?

Cloud-based services are internet-delivered solutions that provide computing resources and applications on demand. Organizations can choose services based on their operational needs and budget requirements.

Cloud services may include:

Website hosting
Application development platforms
Data storage
Networking resources
Complete IT infrastructure

The three primary cloud service models are:

Software as a Service (SaaS)
Platform as a Service (PaaS)
Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)
Software as a Service (SaaS)

SaaS provides users with ready-to-use applications that are accessed through a web browser. The cloud provider manages the servers, databases, and supporting infrastructure behind the application.

Users simply connect to the service online without worrying about maintenance or hardware management.

Common examples include:

Gmail
Slack
Zoom

SaaS is popular because it is convenient, easy to deploy, and requires minimal technical management from customers.

Platform as a Service (PaaS)

PaaS offers online environments and tools that developers use to build, test, and deploy applications. The cloud provider maintains the underlying infrastructure, while developers focus mainly on creating software.

With PaaS, organizations avoid the complexity of managing servers and operating systems themselves.

Examples include:

Google App Engine
Heroku
VMware Cloud Foundry

This model is commonly used for software development and application hosting.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

IaaS provides customers with remote access to computing infrastructure such as:

Virtual servers
Storage systems
Networking components
Processing power

Instead of purchasing physical hardware, organizations rent resources from cloud providers as needed. This makes IaaS a flexible and cost-efficient alternative to maintaining on-premises infrastructure.

Customers typically have greater control over systems in IaaS environments compared to SaaS or PaaS.

Major Cloud Service Providers

Some of the world’s largest technology companies offer cloud computing services, including:

Google Cloud Platform
Microsoft Azure

These providers support businesses ranging from small startups to large enterprises.

What is Cloud Security?

Cloud security is a specialized area of cybersecurity focused on protecting:

Cloud-based applications
Data stored in the cloud
Cloud infrastructure and services

Traditional IT systems were usually maintained entirely within an organization’s physical environment. In those setups, internal security teams handled all security responsibilities directly.

Cloud computing changes this model because infrastructure and services are shared between customers and cloud providers. As a result, security responsibilities are divided between both parties.

Shared Responsibility Model

Cloud security commonly follows the shared responsibility model. This means both the cloud provider and the customer play important roles in securing systems and data.

Typically, customers are responsible for:

Identity and access management
Configuring security settings
Protecting and managing their data

Meanwhile, cloud providers are generally responsible for:

Maintaining physical infrastructure
Securing servers and networking hardware
Ensuring platform availability

The level of responsibility depends on the type of service being used:

SaaS gives customers the least control and responsibility
IaaS gives customers the most control and responsibility
Common Cloud Security Challenges

Although cloud providers invest heavily in security, organizations still face several risks when using cloud environments.

1. Misconfiguration

Incorrect security settings are one of the leading causes of cloud security incidents. Many organizations rely on default configurations that may not properly protect sensitive information.

Example:

Publicly exposed storage systems
Weak access permissions
2. Cloud-Native Breaches

Improperly configured cloud services increase the likelihood of unauthorized access and data breaches.

3. Difficulty Monitoring Access

Tracking user activity and monitoring access across cloud systems can become complicated, especially in large or multi-cloud environments.

4. Regulatory Compliance

Organizations in regulated industries must ensure their cloud environments comply with standards such as:

HIPAA
PCI DSS
GDPR

Meeting these legal and regulatory requirements can be challenging when data is stored across multiple cloud systems or geographic regions.

Growing Demand for Cloud Security

As more organizations move operations to the cloud, the demand for cloud security professionals continues to increase. Businesses need experts who can identify risks, configure secure environments, and protect sensitive cloud-based resources.

Cloud security has become one of the most valuable and in-demand areas within modern cybersecurity because of the rapid growth of cloud adoption worldwide.

Understanding the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

Organizations face many cybersecurity risks, making it difficult to create a complete security strategy from scratch. To help businesses manage these challenges more effectively, cybersecurity frameworks provide structured guidance and best practices.

One of the most widely used frameworks is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF). It is flexible, adaptable, and designed to support organizations of different sizes and industries.

Background of the NIST CSF

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) introduced the Cybersecurity Framework in 2014 to strengthen the protection of critical infrastructure in the United States.

NIST was chosen to create the framework because it is considered a reliable and neutral source of scientific research, standards, and cybersecurity guidance.

Although the framework was initially developed for critical infrastructure sectors, it was later expanded so that both public and private organizations could use it. The goal was to create a practical and flexible approach that even smaller organizations could adopt without needing extensive resources.

Main Components of the CSF

The NIST CSF consists of three major components:

Core
Tiers
Profiles

Each component helps organizations improve and manage their cybersecurity programs.

1. CSF Core

The CSF Core outlines desired cybersecurity outcomes and activities that organizations can use to strengthen security operations.

The core currently includes six primary functions:

Identify
Protect
Detect
Respond
Recover
Govern

These functions help organizations:

Recognize important assets
Apply appropriate protections
Detect cybersecurity incidents
Respond to attacks
Restore operations after incidents
Manage cybersecurity through leadership and governance

The Govern function was added in 2024 to highlight the importance of executive leadership, policy-making, and risk management in cybersecurity programs.

The CSF Core serves as a reference structure that organizations can customize according to their needs.

2. CSF Tiers

The CSF Tiers measure the maturity and sophistication of an organization’s cybersecurity practices.

The tiers range from:

Tier 1 – Limited or basic cybersecurity practices
Tier 4 – Advanced and highly adaptive cybersecurity operations

Organizations use tiers to:

Evaluate their current security posture
Understand how mature their cybersecurity processes are
Identify areas that require improvement

Higher tiers generally indicate stronger risk management and more developed security programs.

3. CSF Profiles

CSF Profiles are customized templates based on the framework. They are designed by cybersecurity and industry experts to address the needs of specific sectors or organizations.

Profiles help organizations:

Build a starting point for cybersecurity planning
Compare their current security posture against industry expectations
Align security efforts with business goals and regulatory requirements

Different industries may use different profiles depending on the risks they commonly face.

Compliance and the CSF

Compliance refers to following required standards, policies, laws, or regulations related to security and data protection.

It is important to understand the difference between:

Regulations – mandatory rules organizations must follow
Frameworks – optional guidelines and best practices organizations may choose to adopt

The NIST CSF is voluntary, but many organizations use it to support compliance with legal and industry requirements.

Using the CSF can help businesses:

Improve cybersecurity operations
Demonstrate responsible risk management
Align with recognized security standards
Challenges of Implementing the CSF

Although the framework is highly beneficial, implementing it can sometimes be difficult because of its complexity and level of detail.

Organizations may face challenges such as:

Understanding where the framework fits into existing security programs
Determining how to begin implementation
Adapting the framework to their operational needs

Some organizations already have established security practices, while others may be building security programs from the beginning. The framework can support both situations, but implementation often requires planning and assessment.

Recommended Steps for Implementing the CSF

The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) provides guidance for organizations adopting the framework. Common implementation steps include:

1. Develop a Current Profile

Review existing cybersecurity operations and identify business requirements.

2. Perform a Risk Assessment

Evaluate risks, vulnerabilities, and how well current controls meet business and regulatory needs.

3. Identify and Prioritize Gaps

Determine weaknesses or missing controls that expose assets to risk.

4. Create and Implement an Action Plan

Develop security improvements that align with organizational objectives and risk management goals.

Organizations should also continuously monitor evolving threats, vulnerabilities, and cybersecurity trends while using the framework.

Benefits of the NIST CSF

The NIST CSF has become widely adopted because it:

Works across many industries
Supports organizations of different sizes
Aligns with established cybersecurity best practices
Helps improve regulatory compliance
Encourages better communication about cybersecurity risks

Its flexibility and broad acceptance make it one of the most valuable cybersecurity frameworks used today.

Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)

Security controls help protect sensitive information and systems from misuse or attacks. One of the most important security controls is the Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP), also known simply as least privilege. This principle means that users are given only the minimum level of access and permissions needed to perform their specific tasks.

Least privilege is a core cybersecurity concept that supports the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) triad by reducing unnecessary access to systems and data. Organizations use this principle to lower security risks, manage users more effectively, and maintain better control over resources.

Why Least Privilege Matters

Applying least privilege helps organizations reduce the chances of security incidents such as data breaches, misuse, or accidental damage. It improves security by:

Restricting access to sensitive information
Preventing accidental modification, deletion, or misuse of data
Improving system monitoring and administrative control

By limiting what users can access and what actions they can perform, organizations reduce the likelihood of successful cyberattacks. Least privilege works best when users and resources are clearly defined and access permissions are carefully managed.

Least privilege is also connected to another important concept called separation of duties, where responsibilities are divided among different individuals so that no single person has complete control over critical operations.

Determining User Access and Authorization

Before implementing least privilege, organizations must answer two important questions:

Who is the user?
What level of access does the user need?

A user can be a person, device, or software application connected to the network. Each user should generally have an individual account managed through the organization’s directory service.

Common Types of User Accounts
Guest accounts: Temporary accounts for external users such as customers, contractors, or business partners
User accounts: Standard accounts assigned to employees based on their job responsibilities
Service accounts: Accounts used by applications or software to communicate with other systems
Privileged accounts: Accounts with elevated or administrative permissions

Organizations usually establish a baseline access level for each account type. However, access needs may change depending on the situation. For example, a support agent may only require temporary access to a customer’s information while actively assisting them.

Least privilege is only effective when user permissions are regularly reviewed and monitored. Strong password practices are also essential because weak passwords can compromise even properly configured accounts.

Auditing User Accounts and Privileges

Regular audits are necessary to ensure that user accounts and permissions remain secure and appropriate over time. Organizations commonly perform three types of audits:

Usage Audits

Usage audits review which resources users access and how they use them. These audits help verify that users are following company security policies and identify permissions that are no longer necessary.

Privilege Audits

Over time, users may accumulate unnecessary permissions, a problem known as privilege creep. This often happens when employees change roles or receive promotions. Privilege audits compare a user’s current responsibilities with their assigned permissions to ensure access remains appropriate.

Account Change Audits

Directory services maintain logs of account-related activities and changes. Account change audits review these records to identify suspicious behavior, such as repeated password change attempts or unauthorized modifications to accounts.

Many directory services can also generate alerts for suspicious account activity, helping administrators respond quickly to potential threats.













The Data Lifecycle and Data Protection

Organizations handle large amounts of sensitive information every day, making data privacy and security extremely important. Data can be vulnerable at different stages, whether it is stored, actively being used, or transferred between systems. To maintain the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) triad, organizations must apply proper security controls throughout the entire lifespan of data.

The Data Lifecycle

Security professionals often use the data lifecycle model to understand how data moves through an organization and how it should be protected at each stage. The lifecycle helps businesses create policies and implement technologies that keep information secure and accessible.

The data lifecycle generally includes five stages:

Collect – Data is gathered from users, systems, or external sources.
Store – Information is saved in databases, servers, or cloud systems.
Use – Data is accessed, processed, or shared for business operations.
Archive – Older data is retained for legal, regulatory, or business purposes.
Destroy – Data that is no longer needed is securely deleted or destroyed.

Protecting data at every stage ensures that information remains available, recoverable, and secure if an incident occurs.

Data Governance

Because organizations constantly collect and manage new information, they require a structured approach to handling data securely. This process is known as data governance.

Data governance refers to the policies, procedures, and processes that define how an organization manages and protects its information throughout the data lifecycle. These policies focus on maintaining data privacy, accuracy, availability, and security.

Key Roles in Data Governance

Organizations commonly assign specific responsibilities to different individuals or groups:

Data Owner: The individual responsible for deciding who can access, use, edit, or delete data.
Data Custodian: The person, department, or system responsible for storing, transporting, and protecting data securely.
Data Steward: The person or team responsible for implementing and maintaining data governance policies.

Security professionals often work as data custodians, ensuring that information is not stolen, damaged, or misused.

Protecting Data Throughout Its Lifecycle

Most organizations create a data governance policy to define how data should be handled across the company. These policies establish security procedures, define access restrictions, and explain how employees should manage sensitive information.

Effective data protection requires limiting access to authorized users only and ensuring that data remains secure at every stage of its lifecycle.

Legally Protected Information

Data often contains personal and sensitive details about individuals. Since this information can reveal someone’s identity, health status, financial details, or personal decisions, organizations must protect it carefully and respect users’ privacy choices.

Governments and regulatory agencies have established laws and standards that require organizations to secure specific types of sensitive data.

Types of Protected Information
Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

PII refers to any information that can identify or locate an individual. Examples include:

Full name
Phone number
Email address
Home address
Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI includes medical and health-related information connected to an individual. In the United States, PHI is regulated by the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA). In the European Union, similar protections are enforced through the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

Examples of PHI include:

Medical records
Health conditions
Treatment history
Insurance information
Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (SPII)

SPII is a more sensitive category of PII that requires stricter protection and limited access on a need-to-know basis.

Examples include:

Bank account numbers
Credit card details
Login credentials
Social Security numbers

Because this information can cause serious harm if exposed, organizations must apply stronger security controls to protect it.















Information Security and Privacy

Security and privacy are closely connected concepts, but they are not the same. Organizations must both protect sensitive information and respect individuals’ rights to control how their personal data is collected and used. Security professionals play an important role in maintaining both privacy and security within an organization.

Information Privacy vs. Information Security

Although the terms are often used interchangeably, they have different meanings:

Information Privacy focuses on protecting personal data from unauthorized collection, use, or sharing.
Information Security (InfoSec) focuses on protecting data in all states—at rest, in use, and in transit—from unauthorized access and threats.
Key Difference

Privacy is about giving individuals control over their personal information and how it is shared.
Security is about protecting that information and enforcing the user’s privacy choices.

For example, a retail company may collect customer information such as age, gender, or location for marketing purposes. Before collecting this information, the company should explain how the data will be used and provide customers with the option to decline sharing their information.

Once consent is given, the organization must implement security controls to protect that data from unauthorized access, misuse, or disclosure. Security measures should also ensure that the privacy choices of users who opt out are respected.

Maintaining both privacy and security is essential for building customer trust and protecting an organization’s reputation.

Why Privacy Matters in Security

Privacy became a major concern in the late 1990s as businesses began collecting, storing, and analyzing large amounts of user data for commercial purposes. Organizations started tracking activities such as online searches, shopping behavior, and browsing history to provide personalized services and advertisements.

As data collection increased, concerns also grew about:

Unauthorized sharing of personal information
Data misuse and abuse
Data theft and security breaches

The more data organizations collect and store, the more attractive they become to cybercriminals. This led to greater public awareness and pressure for stronger privacy protections and security measures.

Over time, companies became more transparent about their data practices and introduced additional security controls. However, without clear legal standards, privacy protections varied widely between organizations.

Important Privacy Regulations

Governments and industry groups created privacy and security regulations to protect individuals’ data and establish standards for organizations. These regulations define how personal information should be collected, stored, processed, and protected.

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

The GDPR is a privacy regulation developed by the European Union (EU). It gives individuals greater control over their personal data, including:

Names
Addresses
Phone numbers
Financial information
Medical records

The GDPR applies to any organization that handles the data of EU citizens or residents, even if the business operates outside Europe.

Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)

PCI DSS is a set of security standards created by major financial organizations to protect payment card information.

Its primary goal is to secure credit and debit card transactions and reduce risks related to:

Data theft
Fraud
Unauthorized access to payment information
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

HIPAA is a United States law that protects sensitive patient health information.

HIPAA requires organizations to safeguard medical data and prohibits the disclosure of health information without the patient’s knowledge and consent.

Although these regulations originated in specific regions or industries, they influence security and privacy practices worldwide.

Security Audits and Assessments

Organizations use audits and assessments to ensure compliance with regulations and evaluate the effectiveness of their security controls.

Security Audit

A security audit is a formal review of an organization’s:

Security controls
Policies
Procedures

Audits compare existing practices against regulatory or organizational requirements to determine compliance.

Security Assessment

A security assessment evaluates how effective current security measures are at defending against threats and vulnerabilities.

For example:

An audit may verify whether multi-factor authentication (MFA) is enabled for administrator accounts.
A security assessment may reveal that users still rely on weak passwords, leading the organization to strengthen authentication requirements further.
Differences Between Audits and Assessments
Security Audit	Security Assessment
Focuses on compliance	Focuses on security effectiveness
Usually performed annually	Performed more frequently
May involve external reviewers	Usually conducted internally
Checks if rules are followed	Evaluates resilience against threats

Both audits and assessments are essential for maintaining strong security practices and protecting user privacy.

Encryption and Cryptography

Protecting digital information is a critical part of cybersecurity. Encryption helps keep data private and secure by converting readable information into an encoded format that unauthorized users cannot understand.

Key Terms
Encryption: The process of converting readable data into encoded data.
Public Key Infrastructure (PKI): A framework that secures online communication using encryption and digital certificates.
Cipher: A mathematical algorithm used to encrypt and decrypt information.

Encryption is widely used to secure data during storage, transmission, and processing.

Types of Encryption

There are two primary types of encryption used in cybersecurity:

Symmetric Encryption

Symmetric encryption uses a single secret key for both encryption and decryption. The sender and receiver must both possess the same key to securely exchange information.

Characteristics:
Faster processing speed
Efficient for large amounts of data
Requires secure key sharing
Asymmetric Encryption

Asymmetric encryption uses two separate keys:

A public key to encrypt data
A private key to decrypt data

The private key is only available to authorized users, making this method highly secure.

Characteristics:
More secure for communication over networks
Slower than symmetric encryption
Commonly used for authentication and secure key exchange
Importance of Key Length

Encryption algorithms rely on cryptographic keys. The length of a key directly affects security strength.

Longer keys:

Increase the number of possible combinations
Make brute force attacks significantly harder
Provide stronger protection

However, longer keys also require more processing power and can slow down system performance. Security professionals must balance security and efficiency when selecting encryption methods.

Common Encryption Algorithms

Organizations use both symmetric and asymmetric algorithms to protect sensitive data.

Symmetric Algorithms
Triple DES (3DES)

Triple DES evolved from the older Data Encryption Standard (DES). DES originally used a 56-bit encryption key. Triple DES improves security by applying the DES algorithm three times with three separate keys, creating an effective key length of 168 bits.

Although more secure than DES, Triple DES is becoming outdated because of performance limitations and restrictions on how much data can be encrypted safely. Some organizations still use it for compatibility with older systems.

Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)

AES is one of the most trusted and widely used encryption standards today. It supports key lengths of:

128-bit
192-bit
256-bit

AES is considered extremely secure and resistant to brute force attacks. Modern systems commonly use AES to protect sensitive information.

Asymmetric Algorithms
Rivest Shamir Adleman (RSA)

RSA is one of the earliest and most widely used asymmetric encryption algorithms. It generates both public and private keys and supports key lengths such as:

1,024-bit
2,048-bit
4,096-bit

RSA is commonly used to secure highly sensitive data and establish secure online communication.

Digital Signature Algorithm (DSA)

DSA is a NIST-developed asymmetric algorithm introduced in the 1990s. It commonly uses 2,048-bit keys and is often used alongside RSA in public key infrastructure systems to verify digital signatures and authentication.

Generating Cryptographic Keys

Organizations use tools to generate encryption keys and certificates. One popular tool is OpenSSL, an open-source command-line utility commonly used to create public and private keys and verify digital certificates.

In 2014, OpenSSL experienced a major vulnerability known as the Heartbleed bug, which exposed sensitive data from system memory. The issue was later patched, highlighting the importance of regularly updating software to maintain security.

Kerckhoff’s Principle

According to Kerckhoff’s principle, a cryptographic system should remain secure even if the encryption algorithm is publicly known. The only secret element should be the private key.

This means security should never depend on hiding how an encryption method works. Strong algorithms like AES remain secure despite their designs being publicly available.

Custom or secret encryption methods are often less secure because they have not been thoroughly tested by the cybersecurity community.

Encryption in Everyday Use

Modern systems frequently combine symmetric and asymmetric encryption to balance security and performance.

For example:

Websites often use asymmetric encryption to protect login credentials and establish secure connections.
Once the connection is established, symmetric encryption is used for the rest of the session because it processes data more quickly.

Encryption is also required by many regulations and standards, including:

FIPS 140-3
GDPR

These regulations help ensure organizations handle customer data responsibly and securely.

Hash Functions and Data Integrity

Hash functions are an essential part of modern cybersecurity and are widely used for authentication and non-repudiation. Non-repudiation means that the authenticity of data or actions cannot later be denied.

A hash function is a mathematical algorithm that converts data into a fixed-size value known as a hash or digest. Unlike encryption, hash values are designed to be one-way, meaning they cannot be decrypted back into the original information. Hashing is commonly used to verify data integrity and detect unauthorized changes.

Origins of Hashing

Hash functions were originally developed to improve the speed and efficiency of searching for information in computer systems. Instead of storing and comparing large amounts of data directly, computers could reference smaller fixed-size values using a structure called a hash table.

One of the earliest and most well-known hashing algorithms was Message Digest 5 (MD5), created by Ronald Rivest at MIT in the early 1990s. MD5 was designed to verify file integrity by generating a unique hash value for data.

MD5 converts any input into a 128-bit hash value, typically displayed as a 32-character string. Even the smallest change to the original data creates a completely different hash value.

In general, longer hash values provide stronger security because they reduce the likelihood of duplicate outputs.

Hash Collisions

A major weakness of MD5 is its vulnerability to hash collisions.

A hash collision occurs when two different inputs generate the same hash value. Since hash functions convert unlimited possible inputs into a limited number of outputs, collisions are theoretically unavoidable.

Because MD5 produces relatively short 128-bit hashes, attackers can intentionally create collisions and use them in attacks. This can allow malicious data to appear authentic by matching the same hash value as legitimate data.

Due to these weaknesses, MD5 is no longer considered secure for many modern security applications.

Secure Hash Algorithms (SHA)

To address the weaknesses of MD5, stronger hashing algorithms known as the Secure Hash Algorithms (SHA) family were developed and approved by NIST.

SHA algorithms generate larger hash values, making collisions significantly more difficult.

The SHA family includes:

SHA-1
SHA-224
SHA-256
SHA-384
SHA-512

The number in each name refers to the size of the hash value in bits.

Although SHA-1 produces a 160-bit digest, it is no longer considered secure against collision attacks. More advanced algorithms such as SHA-256 and SHA-512 are commonly used today because they are much more resistant to attacks.

Secure Password Storage

Organizations often store usernames and passwords in databases for authentication purposes. Storing passwords in plaintext creates a major security risk because attackers who gain access to the database can immediately view all credentials.

Hashing improves password security because hash values cannot easily be reversed back into the original password.

When a user logs in:

The entered password is hashed.
The system compares the generated hash with the stored hash value.
Access is granted only if the hashes match.

This approach protects passwords even if the database is compromised.

Rainbow Tables

A rainbow table is a database containing pre-generated hash values and their corresponding plaintext passwords.

Attackers use rainbow tables to quickly compare stolen password hashes against known values in order to recover weak passwords.

Weak or commonly used passwords are especially vulnerable to rainbow table attacks.

Salting Passwords

To strengthen password security, organizations often use salting.

A salt is a random string of characters added to data before hashing. The salt creates a more unique hash value, even when multiple users have the same password.

For example:

Without salting, two users with the password "password" would produce identical hashes.
With salting, each password generates a completely different hash value.

Salting makes rainbow table attacks far less effective because attackers cannot rely on pre-generated hash databases.

Longer and more complex salts provide stronger protection and are now commonly used in secure password storage systems.

Single Sign-On (SSO) and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Organizations rely heavily on authentication systems to protect sensitive information and control user access. Usernames and passwords are commonly used to verify identities, but they are not always enough to secure systems effectively. To strengthen authentication and improve security, many organizations use technologies such as Single Sign-On (SSO) and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).

A Better Approach to Authentication
Single Sign-On (SSO)

Single Sign-On (SSO) is an authentication technology that allows users to access multiple applications or services using one set of login credentials.

Many organizations use SSO because it provides several important benefits:

Improves the user experience by reducing the number of usernames and passwords users must remember
Simplifies account and service management for organizations
Reduces the number of login points attackers can target

SSO was introduced in the 1990s to address password fatigue, which occurs when users become overwhelmed by managing many passwords. Password fatigue often causes people to reuse passwords across different services, increasing security risks.

SSO helps solve this issue by centralizing authentication and reducing the burden on users.

How SSO Works

SSO works by establishing trust between users and service providers through trusted identity systems.

Instead of each application directly verifying the user, an identity provider confirms the user's identity and sends an encrypted access token to the service provider. This token allows the user to access multiple services without logging in repeatedly.

Common SSO Protocols

SSO systems commonly use these authentication protocols:

LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol): Primarily used for on-premises authentication and directory services
SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language): Commonly used for cloud-based and off-premises authentication

LDAP and SAML are often used together in enterprise environments.

Through SSO, one authenticated login session can provide secure access to multiple connected applications and systems.

Limitations of SSO

Although SSO improves convenience and security, it still depends heavily on a single set of credentials.

If an attacker steals or compromises a user’s password, they may gain access to multiple systems connected through SSO. Because of this risk, organizations often combine SSO with stronger authentication methods.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
What is MFA?

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is a security process that requires users to verify their identity using two or more authentication factors before gaining access to a system.

A common example is using an ATM:

A debit card acts as one authentication factor.
A PIN number acts as the second factor.

Only after both factors are verified can the user access their account.

Authentication Factors

MFA strengthens security by requiring multiple forms of identification. These factors generally fall into three categories:

Something the User Knows
Password
PIN
Security question
Something the User Has
Smartphone
Security token
One-time passcode (OTP)
Something the User Is
Fingerprint
Facial recognition
Retina scan

Organizations may implement:

2FA (Two-Factor Authentication): Uses two factors
3FA (Three-Factor Authentication): Uses three factors
Why MFA is Important

MFA significantly improves security because attackers are less likely to possess multiple authentication factors at the same time.

This is especially important in cloud environments, where users often access systems remotely. Even if a password is stolen, attackers may still be unable to bypass additional authentication steps such as OTPs or biometric verification.

By combining SSO and MFA, organizations can improve both usability and security while reducing the risk of unauthorized access.

Identity and Access Management (IAM)

Security is not only about using security tools and technologies. It also involves creating a secure environment where users can safely access the resources they need while reducing the risk of misuse or unauthorized access. Two important security principles that help organizations manage access are:

Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP): Users are given only the minimum access and permissions necessary to perform their tasks.
Separation of Duties (SoD): Responsibilities and permissions are divided among multiple individuals so that no single person has excessive control over critical systems or processes.

These principles often work together. For example, an employee responsible for approving IT purchases should not also have permission to create or process those purchases. Least privilege limits user access, while separation of duties distributes responsibilities to reduce the risk of fraud, abuse, or mistakes.

Identity and Access Management (IAM)

As organizations rely more heavily on digital systems, they must demonstrate that they are protecting sensitive information and controlling access properly. Identity and Access Management (IAM) is a collection of processes and technologies used to manage digital identities and control access to organizational resources.

IAM and the Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting (AAA) framework share similar goals:

Verify user identities
Control user permissions
Track user activities within systems

IAM systems help ensure that:

The right user
Has access to the right resource
At the right time
For the right reason

In IAM, a user can refer to a person, device, or software application.

Authenticating Users

Authentication is the process of verifying that a user is who they claim to be. Organizations commonly use three authentication factors:

Knowledge

Something the user knows, such as:

Passwords
PINs
Security questions
Ownership

Something the user possesses, such as:

Smartphones
Security tokens
One-time passcodes (OTPs)
Characteristic

Something the user is, such as:

Fingerprints
Facial recognition
Retina scans

Organizations often strengthen authentication using technologies such as:

Single Sign-On (SSO): Allows users to access multiple services with one login
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Requires users to verify identity using two or more authentication methods

These methods improve both security and user convenience.

User Provisioning and Deprovisioning

Before authentication systems can function correctly, users must be properly provisioned.

User Provisioning

User provisioning is the process of creating and managing a user’s digital identity and access rights.

For example:

A university may create an instructor account that provides access only to teaching-related systems and resources.

Security analysts are often responsible for configuring user accounts and assigning appropriate permissions.

User Deprovisioning

Deprovisioning removes a user’s access rights when they no longer require access, such as when:

An employee leaves the organization
A contractor’s project ends
A user changes roles

Proper deprovisioning is essential for preventing unauthorized access.

Authorization Frameworks

After a user is authenticated, authorization determines which resources they can access. Organizations commonly use three authorization models:

Mandatory Access Control (MAC)

MAC is the strictest access control model. Access permissions are centrally controlled by administrators and granted only on a need-to-know basis.

Commonly used in:

Military organizations
Government agencies
Law enforcement environments

Users cannot decide access permissions themselves.

Discretionary Access Control (DAC)

In DAC, the data owner decides who can access resources.

Example:

A user sharing a cloud storage folder with view or edit permissions

DAC offers flexibility but can introduce security risks if permissions are shared improperly.

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

RBAC assigns permissions based on a user’s role within the organization.

Examples:

Marketing employees may access analytics systems
IT administrators may access network management tools

RBAC simplifies permission management and supports least privilege practices.

Access Control Technologies

Authentication and authorization are often combined into a seamless user experience through integrated access control technologies.

A typical IAM or AAA solution may include:

User directories
Identity management tools
Authorization systems
Auditing and monitoring systems

Some organizations build custom access control systems, while others use third-party IAM solutions that provide ready-made security tools and automation features.

These technologies help:

Reduce administrative workload
Improve accuracy
Lower security risks
Monitor and update permissions efficiently

However, effective security depends not only on using these tools, but also on configuring and managing them properly.

Vulnerability Scanners and Vulnerability Scanning

Previously, you learned that a vulnerability assessment is an internal process used by organizations to review the security of their systems. The purpose of these assessments is to identify weaknesses before attackers can exploit them.

One of the most important tools used during this process is a vulnerability scanner. These tools help security teams detect weaknesses, simulate potential threats, and support remediation efforts before a real attack occurs.

What is a Vulnerability Scanner?

A vulnerability scanner is a software tool that automatically checks systems and technologies for known vulnerabilities and exposures.

These scanners compare systems against databases containing:

Known vulnerabilities
Security weaknesses
Misconfigurations
Programming flaws

Their main goal is to identify weaknesses that could be exploited by attackers.

Attack Surfaces Scanned by Vulnerability Tools

Vulnerability scanners can analyze different layers of an organization’s attack surface as part of a defense-in-depth strategy.

1. Perimeter Layer

This includes systems that control external access, such as:

Authentication systems
Access gateways

Scanners evaluate whether unauthorized access could occur.

2. Network Layer

This layer includes networking technologies such as:

Firewalls
Routers
Network security devices

Scanning tools check for exposed ports, weak configurations, and other network vulnerabilities.

3. Endpoint Layer

Endpoints are devices connected to the network, including:

Laptops
Desktop computers
Servers

Scanners analyze these devices for outdated software, missing patches, and configuration weaknesses.

4. Application Layer

This layer includes software and applications users interact with directly.

Scanners look for:

Coding flaws
Input validation weaknesses
Security vulnerabilities within applications
5. Data Layer

The data layer includes information:

At rest (stored data)
In transit (moving across networks)
In use (actively processed data)

Scanners help identify risks related to data exposure and insecure handling practices.

How Vulnerability Scanners Work

When a scan begins, the scanner:

Examines systems and services
Compares findings against vulnerability databases
Identifies known weaknesses
Flags detected vulnerabilities

The findings are then added to the scanner’s reference database, improving future detection and analysis accuracy.

Important Note:

The vulnerability databases used by scanning tools are regularly updated by vendors to include newly discovered threats and vulnerabilities.

Performing Vulnerability Scans

Vulnerability scanners are designed to be non-intrusive.

This means:

They identify weaknesses without exploiting them
They simulate potential attack paths safely
They do not intentionally damage systems like real attackers would
Important Note:

Although scanners are generally safe, scans can occasionally cause unintended problems, such as slowing down or crashing unstable systems.

Types of Vulnerability Scans

Different scan types simulate different attacker behaviors and access levels.

External vs Internal Scans
External Scans

External scans simulate attacks originating outside the organization’s network.

Focus:
Public-facing systems
Websites
Firewalls
Internet-accessible services
Goal:

Identify weaknesses visible to external attackers, such as:

Open ports
Vulnerable servers
Weak perimeter defenses

Internal scans examine systems from inside the organization’s network.

Focus:
Internal applications
Internal systems
Employee-accessible resources
Goal:

Detect vulnerabilities that could be exploited by insiders or attackers who already gained network access.

Example:

Weak input handling in internal applications
Authenticated vs Unauthenticated Scans
Authenticated Scans

Authenticated scans use valid login credentials during testing.

Access levels may include:
Standard user accounts
Administrative accounts
Purpose:
Identify vulnerabilities only visible after login
Detect broken access controls
Assess user permissions and privilege issues

Unauthenticated scans simulate attackers with no legitimate access.

Purpose:

Databases
Applications

Software Updates and Patch Management in Cybersecurity

You may have noticed that computers, smartphones, and applications frequently require updates. While updates often improve performance, add features, or fix bugs, they are also extremely important for cybersecurity.

From a security perspective, updates help organizations fix vulnerabilities that could expose users, systems, and networks to cyber threats.

Updates are an important part of every organization’s remediation strategy, which is the process of addressing weaknesses identified during vulnerability assessments.

Why Security Updates Matter

Outdated systems are similar to buildings with unlocked doors. Attackers look for weaknesses in old software the same way intruders look for unsecured entry points.

Software updates help close these security gaps before they can be exploited.

What is a Patch Update?

A patch update is a software or operating system update designed to fix:

Security vulnerabilities
Software bugs
Stability problems

Patches are mainly released to address known weaknesses and common vulnerabilities that attackers may exploit.

Zero-Day Vulnerabilities

Sometimes patches are released in response to a zero-day vulnerability.

A zero-day vulnerability is:

A previously unknown security flaw
Exploited by attackers before developers are aware of it or before a fix exists

In such cases, developers work quickly to release emergency security patches.

Common Update Strategies

Organizations and users generally install updates using two main approaches:

Manual updates
Automatic updates

Each method has advantages and disadvantages.

Manual Updates

With a manual update strategy, users or IT teams are responsible for:

Finding updates
Downloading them
Installing them manually

In enterprise environments, configuration management tools are often used to deploy updates to selected systems or groups of users.

Advantages of Manual Updates
Greater Control

Organizations can:

Test updates before deployment
Avoid unstable or problematic patches
Decide when and where updates are installed

This is useful if vendors release poorly tested updates.

Disadvantages of Manual Updates
Risk of Missing Critical Updates

Manual processes can lead to:

Forgotten updates
Delayed patching
Systems remaining vulnerable for long periods
Automatic Updates

Automatic updates allow systems or applications to:

Detect available updates
Download patches automatically
Install updates without manual intervention

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) recommends enabling automatic updates whenever possible.

Advantages of Automatic Updates
Faster Protection

Automatic updates:

Keep systems current
Reduce exposure to vulnerabilities
Simplify patch deployment

Critical security patches can be applied quickly across systems.

Disadvantages of Automatic Updates
Potential Stability Issues

If vendors release poorly tested patches, automatic deployment can cause:

System instability
Performance issues
Compatibility problems

This may negatively affect user experience or business operations.

End-of-Life (EOL) Software

All software products have a lifecycle.

Eventually, vendors stop supporting older versions when newer versions are released. This unsupported software is called End-of-Life (EOL) software.

Risks of EOL Software

Although the software may still function, vendors no longer provide:

Security updates
Bug fixes
Technical support

This creates serious security risks because newly discovered vulnerabilities remain permanently unpatched.

CISA Recommendation

CISA recommends organizations discontinue the use of EOL software whenever possible because it creates long-term, unfixable security exposure.

However, many businesses continue using outdated systems due to:

High replacement costs
Operational dependencies
Compatibility requirements
Difference Between Patches and Upgrades
Patch
Small update that fixes vulnerabilities or bugs
Keeps the same software version
Upgrade
A completely new version of hardware or software
Often includes major changes or new features
IoT and Growing Security Risks

The risks associated with unpatched and outdated systems continue to increase as more devices become internet-connected.

Examples include Internet of Things (IoT) devices such as:

Smart bulbs
Security cameras
Smart thermostats
Connected appliances
Why This is Dangerous

Even one vulnerable device can become an entry point for attackers.

In organizational environments, a single unpatched IoT device may allow attackers to:

Gain access to internal networks
Move laterally through systems
Launch larger attacks




















Penetration Testing in Cybersecurity

A strong cybersecurity strategy depends on regularly testing systems to identify weaknesses before attackers can exploit them. Earlier, you learned that organizations perform vulnerability assessments to review the security of their systems and identify weaknesses.

Another important security practice is penetration testing, which helps organizations evaluate how effective their defenses are against real-world attacks.

What is Penetration Testing?

A penetration test, commonly called a pen test, is a simulated cyberattack performed to identify and exploit vulnerabilities in:

Systems
Networks
Applications
Websites
Security processes

The goal is to imitate the methods and techniques used by real attackers in order to understand how a system would respond during an actual attack.

Because these tests are officially authorized, penetration testing is considered a form of ethical hacking.

Difference Between Vulnerability Assessments and Penetration Testing
Vulnerability Assessment
Identifies weaknesses in systems
Focuses on detecting vulnerabilities
Penetration Testing
Actively exploits vulnerabilities
Determines the potential impact of a successful attack

In simple terms:

Vulnerability assessments find weaknesses
Penetration tests show what attackers could do with those weaknesses
Example of Penetration Testing

A financial institution might perform a penetration test on its online banking application to determine whether attackers could:

Access customer information
Transfer funds illegally
Exploit misconfigured security settings

If weaknesses are discovered, the organization can correct them and strengthen the application’s security.

Compliance and Penetration Testing

Many organizations are legally or contractually required to conduct regular penetration testing to meet compliance standards.

Examples include:

PCI DSS
HIPAA
GDPR

Regular testing helps organizations maintain regulatory compliance and improve security readiness.

Types of Penetration Testing Teams

Organizations use different penetration testing approaches depending on their security objectives.

Red Team

Red teams simulate realistic attacks against systems, applications, or networks.

Goal:
Identify vulnerabilities
Test how well defenses can resist attacks

Red teams act like real attackers.

Blue Team

Blue teams focus on defense and incident response.

Responsibilities:
Detect attacks
Respond to threats
Defend systems and networks

Blue teams evaluate the effectiveness of existing security controls.

Purple Team

Purple teams combine the efforts of red and blue teams.

Goal:
Improve collaboration
Strengthen the organization’s overall security posture

Purple team exercises help both attackers and defenders learn from each other.

Who Performs Penetration Tests?

Penetration tests are usually conducted by skilled professionals with expertise in:

Networking
Programming
System architecture
Cybersecurity tools and techniques

Organizations may:

Hire independent ethical hackers
Use internal penetration testing teams

Before testing begins, pen testers decide how much system access and information they will receive.

Penetration Testing Strategies

There are three common penetration testing methods based on the level of information provided to the tester.

1. Open-Box Testing (White-Box Testing)

In open-box testing, the tester is given extensive access and knowledge about the system.

Information may include:
Network diagrams
System architecture
Source code
Data flow details
Other names:
White-box testing
Clear-box testing
Full-knowledge testing
Benefit:

Allows deep analysis of internal security weaknesses.

2. Closed-Box Testing (Black-Box Testing)

In closed-box testing, the tester receives little or no internal information.

Goal:

Simulate a real external attacker.

Other names:
Black-box testing
Zero-knowledge testing
External testing
Benefit:

Provides realistic attack simulations.

3. Partial-Knowledge Testing (Gray-Box Testing)

In gray-box testing, the tester receives limited information and access.

Example:

Testing from the perspective of a regular employee or customer service representative.

Other names:
Gray-box testing
Benefit:

Simulates insider threats or limited-access attackers.

Why Different Testing Approaches Matter

Each testing strategy provides unique insights:

Open-box testing reveals deep internal flaws
Closed-box testing simulates realistic external attacks
Gray-box testing evaluates risks from limited-access users

Together, they help organizations better understand potential attack paths.

Skills Needed for Penetration Testing

Penetration testers require strong technical and analytical skills.

Important areas include:

Network security
Application security
Linux and operating systems
Vulnerability analysis
Threat modeling
Detection and response tools
Communication skills

Programming knowledge is especially valuable because many tests involve software systems and automation.

Commonly used languages include:

Python
Bash
Becoming a Penetration Tester

Penetration testing is a rapidly growing cybersecurity career field.

Even entry-level cybersecurity professionals can gradually build the skills needed through:

Practice
Hands-on labs
Security research
Ethical hacking exercises

Many organizations run bug bounty programs that reward ethical hackers for finding and responsibly reporting vulnerabilities.

These programs provide:

Real-world experience
Opportunities to improve technical skills
Financial rewards for valid findings
HackerOne

HackerOne is a popular platform where ethical hackers can participate in bug bounty programs and security testing activities.

Applying an Attacker Mindset in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is constantly evolving. New threats, attack methods, and technologies can quickly change the security landscape, making adaptability an essential skill for security professionals. Because of this fast-paced environment, organizations must prepare for potential risks before they become real attacks.

One of the first steps in staying prepared is identifying vulnerabilities. Earlier, you learned that a vulnerability assessment is an internal review process used to discover weaknesses in an organization’s security systems. However, simply identifying vulnerabilities is not enough. Security professionals must also analyze those weaknesses from the perspective of an attacker to understand how they could be exploited.

Preparing for Potential Threats

Having a response plan is important, but organizations must first determine what threats they should prepare for. To accomplish this, security teams often perform simulations as part of their vulnerability management process.

These simulations involve adopting an attacker mindset, which means thinking like a threat actor in order to understand how systems could be targeted or compromised.

Applying an attacker mindset is similar to conducting a controlled experiment:

Security teams intentionally test weaknesses
They observe how defenses respond
They analyze the results to improve security strategies

This perspective helps organizations better understand risks and develop stronger protection measures.

Simulating Cyber Threats

A common way to apply an attacker mindset is through attack simulations. These simulations are generally performed using two approaches:

Proactive simulations
Reactive simulations

Both approaches aim to strengthen security systems, but they focus on different objectives.

Proactive Simulations (Red Team Exercises)

Proactive simulations imitate the behavior of attackers by attempting to exploit vulnerabilities and bypass defenses.

These activities are commonly known as red team exercises.

Purpose:
Identify weaknesses before real attackers do
Test how effective security defenses are
Simulate realistic attack scenarios

Red teams often spend significant time planning attacks before executing them.

Example:

A team might send fake phishing emails to employees to evaluate whether staff members reveal login credentials or sensitive information.

Reactive Simulations (Blue Team Exercises)

Reactive simulations focus on defense and incident response.

These are often referred to as blue team exercises.

Purpose:
Detect attacks
Respond to threats
Investigate vulnerabilities
Improve defensive capabilities

Blue teams usually gather information about systems and assets using security tools such as vulnerability scanners.

Vulnerability Scanning in Simulations

A vulnerability scanner is software that automatically compares systems against known vulnerabilities and exposures.

Security teams use scanners to:

Detect weaknesses in networks and systems
Identify outdated software
Find security misconfigurations

Reactive simulations frequently rely on scan results to evaluate risks and determine appropriate remediation steps.

Example of a Reactive Security Simulation

A security team performing an external vulnerability scan may follow several steps:

1. Identification

A vulnerable system is discovered.

Example:

A server is identified as running an outdated operating system.

2. Vulnerability Analysis

The team researches:

Known vulnerabilities associated with the outdated system
Possible exploitation methods
Available security patches or fixes
3. Risk Assessment

The organization evaluates:

Severity of the vulnerability
Potential business impact
Likelihood of exploitation

This helps prioritize remediation efforts.

4. Remediation

Security teams apply corrective actions, such as:

Installing updates
Changing configurations
Strengthening security controls

The goal is to eliminate or reduce the identified risk.

Reporting and Communication

After simulations and assessments are completed, security teams often create detailed reports describing:

Identified vulnerabilities
Risk levels
Recommended remediation steps
Testing outcomes

These reports may be shared with:

Management
IT teams
Service providers

Clear communication is an important skill in cybersecurity because decision-makers rely on accurate reports to improve security strategies.

The Importance of Innovative Security Thinking

Many modern security controls were created in response to real-world attacks. Since cybercriminals continuously develop new ways to bypass defenses, security professionals must constantly adapt and stay informed.

Effectively applying an attacker mindset requires:

Awareness of emerging threats
Knowledge of current vulnerabilities
Understanding of evolving attack techniques
Staying Current with Security Trends

Cybersecurity professionals regularly use trusted resources to stay informed about vulnerabilities and threats.

One important resource is the:

NIST National Vulnerability Database (NVD)

The NVD provides information about:

Common vulnerabilities
Security weaknesses

Vulnerability scoring

















Threat Actors and the Attack Surface (Reframed)

Anticipating cyberattacks is a key skill for any security professional. Developing this ability requires a flexible mindset and awareness of where attacks can originate. Earlier, you learned about the attack surface, which includes all possible points where a system can be targeted or exploited.

Common attack surfaces include:

Networks
Servers
Devices
Employees and staff

As organizations become more digitally connected, these attack surfaces continue to grow. A fundamental security principle is to reduce exposure and limit access wherever possible.

What are Threat Actors?

A threat actor is any individual or group that can pose a risk to an organization’s security. This includes both intentional attackers and individuals who may accidentally create security risks.

Threat actors can exist both:

Inside an organization
Outside an organization
Categories of Threat Actors (Based on Motivation)
1. Competitors

These are rival organizations that may attempt to gain an advantage by stealing sensitive or confidential information.

2. State Actors

These are government-backed groups or intelligence agencies that conduct cyber operations for national interests.

3. Criminal Syndicates

These are organized groups involved in cybercrime, often motivated by financial gain.

4. Insider Threats

Insider threats include anyone who has or previously had authorized access to systems.

This category includes:

Employees who accidentally cause security issues
Individuals who intentionally misuse access for personal benefit
5. Shadow IT

Shadow IT refers to the use of unauthorized tools or systems without official IT approval.

Example:

Employees using personal email accounts for work communication
Hackers and Their Roles

A hacker is any individual who uses technical skills to gain access to computer systems, networks, or data. However, this term is broad and can describe different intentions.

Types of Hackers
1. Unauthorized (Malicious) Hackers

These individuals use their skills for illegal or harmful purposes.

Also known as unethical or black-hat hackers
Skill levels vary widely
Some rely on pre-built tools or scripts (often called script kiddies)
2. Authorized (Ethical) Hackers

These hackers use their skills to improve security.

They include:

Internal security teams
External security consultants
Bug bounty participants who report vulnerabilities responsibly

Their goal is to identify weaknesses before attackers do.

3. Semi-Authorized Hackers

These individuals operate in a gray area.

Examples include:

Hacktivists who use hacking for political or social causes

They may expose vulnerabilities to raise awareness rather than cause direct harm.

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Most attackers attempt quick intrusions and then exit systems. However, some attackers remain hidden within systems for long periods.

These are known as Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs).

What is an APT?

An APT is a long-term cyberattack in which a threat actor:

Gains unauthorized access
Remains undetected for an extended period
Continuously collects information
Characteristics of APTs

Focused on surveillance and intelligence gathering
Target critical sectors such as:
Government

Even private companies may be targeted first as stepping stones toward larger objectives.

Supply chain systems and vendor access points
Why Understanding Threat Actors Matters

Different threat actors use different strategies depending on their goals and access methods.

For example:

Understanding motivations and access methods helps security teams:

Predict attack patterns
Strengthen defenses
Reduce exposure across the attack surface

Brute Force Attacks and Password Security

Usernames and passwords remain some of the most widely used security controls today. They act as digital locks that protect access to systems, networks, applications, and sensitive information. However, relying only on login credentials can create security risks because attackers frequently attempt to steal, guess, or crack passwords.

One common attack method used against login systems is the brute force attack, which relies on repeated trial-and-error attempts to discover private information such as usernames, passwords, or encryption keys.

Understanding Brute Force Attacks

A brute force attack is similar to trying every possible key combination to open a locked door. Threat actors repeatedly test different credential combinations until they successfully gain access to a system.

These attacks can target:

User accounts
Applications
Networks
Encrypted data

Because login systems are commonly exposed to the internet, they are frequent targets for brute force attempts.

Common Types of Brute Force Attacks

Threat actors use several methods when attempting to crack credentials or bypass authentication systems.

Simple Brute Force Attacks

In a simple brute force attack, attackers repeatedly guess usernames and passwords until they find valid credentials.

Examples include:

Trying common passwords
Testing random combinations
Guessing weak passwords manually or automatically

This method relies entirely on repeated guessing attempts.

Dictionary Attacks

A dictionary attack uses a precompiled list of commonly used passwords and usernames instead of randomly generating combinations.

Attackers may use:

Common passwords
Frequently leaked credentials
Predictable word combinations

This approach is faster than basic brute force guessing because it focuses on passwords users commonly create.

Reverse Brute Force Attacks

A reverse brute force attack begins with one known password or credential and tests it across multiple accounts or systems.

For example:

Attackers may use one stolen password against thousands of usernames

This method is effective when users reuse passwords across multiple services.

Credential Stuffing

Credential stuffing occurs when attackers use usernames and passwords stolen from previous data breaches to access accounts on other systems.

Since many users reuse credentials across websites, attackers often succeed if password reuse exists.

Pass-the-Hash Attacks

A specialized form of credential stuffing is called a pass-the-hash attack.

Instead of using plaintext passwords, attackers use stolen hashed credentials to trick authentication systems into granting access without cracking the original password.

Exhaustive Key Search

Brute force methods can also target encrypted information using a technique known as exhaustive key search.

In this attack:

Attackers systematically try every possible encryption key until the correct one is found

This process can require enormous computing power and time depending on encryption strength.

Brute Force Tools

Manually attempting every possible credential combination would take an extremely long time. Because of this, attackers commonly use automated software tools to speed up brute force attacks.

Common brute force tools include:

Aircrack-ng
Hashcat
John the Ripper
Ophcrack
THC Hydra

These tools automate password guessing, credential testing, and hash cracking.

Ethical Use of Brute Force Tools

Security professionals may also use these tools for legitimate security testing.

For example:

Testing password strength
Auditing authentication systems
Evaluating Wi-Fi security

A tool like Aircrack-ng may be used to assess whether a wireless network is vulnerable to password cracking attacks.

Preventing Brute Force Attacks

Organizations reduce the risk of brute force attacks by combining technical and administrative security controls.

Common prevention methods include:

Hashing and salting
Multi-factor authentication (MFA)
CAPTCHA systems
Strong password policies
Hashing and Salting
Hashing

Hashing converts data, such as passwords, into a fixed unique value called a hash.

This process helps:

Protect stored passwords
Verify data integrity
Salting

Salting strengthens hashing by adding random characters to passwords before hashing them.

Benefits include:

Increased complexity
Protection against dictionary attacks
Greater resistance to brute force attempts

Salting makes identical passwords produce different hash values.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Multi-factor authentication requires users to verify their identity using two or more authentication methods.

Examples include:

Passwords
Mobile verification codes
Fingerprints
Security tokens

MFA improves security because attackers would need multiple forms of authentication even if a password becomes compromised.

CAPTCHA Systems

CAPTCHA stands for:

Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart

CAPTCHA systems help distinguish human users from automated attack tools.

Common CAPTCHA Types
Text-Based CAPTCHA

Users must identify distorted letters or numbers and enter them correctly.

Image-Based CAPTCHA

Users must select images matching a requested object or phrase.

Examples:

Traffic lights
Bicycles
Crosswalks

CAPTCHA systems help slow down or block automated brute force attempts.

Password Policies

Organizations use password policies to enforce secure password practices across users and systems.

Common password policy requirements include:

Minimum password length
Combination of letters, numbers, and symbols
Password expiration schedules
Unique password requirements
Account lockout rules after failed login attempts
Account Lockout Policies

Many systems temporarily suspend accounts after multiple failed login attempts.

This helps:

Prevent unlimited guessing attempts
Slow brute force attacks
Alert administrators to suspicious activity
Why Strong Password Policies Matter

Strong password policies increase:

Password complexity
Number of possible combinations
Time required for attackers to crack credentials

The more possible combinations that exist, the harder brute force attacks become.

NIST Password Guidance

Organizations often reference password security recommendations from the:

NIST Special Publication 800-63B

This publication provides guidance on:

Password management
Authentication security
Identity verification best practices
Final Summary

Brute force attacks rely on repeated attempts to guess passwords, credentials, or encryption keys. Threat actors use methods such as:

Dictionary attacks
Credential stuffing
Reverse brute force attacks
Pass-the-hash attacks

To defend against these threats, organizations implement layered security measures including:

Hashing and salting
MFA
CAPTCHA systems
Strong password policies

Together, these controls help protect systems, accounts, and sensitive data from unauthorized access.
